# Best Yarns for Beginners: A Knitter's Honest Guide

*By Inna | Purl of Wisdom*

**Target keyword:** best yarns for beginners
**Secondary keywords:** beginner knitting yarn, easy yarn for learning to knit, what yarn should I start with, best yarn for first knitting project

---

Choosing your first yarn can feel overwhelming. Walk into any yarn shop — or scroll through an online store — and you'll find hundreds of options in every fibre, weight, and colour imaginable. How do you pick the right one when you don't know what half the labels mean yet?

Here's the good news: the "best" beginner yarn isn't one specific brand. It's any yarn that makes learning easier and more enjoyable. In this guide, I'll walk you through exactly what to look for, what to avoid, and share my tried-and-tested recommendations.

## What Makes a Yarn Good for Beginners?

Before we get to specific picks, let's talk about what actually matters when you're learning to knit.

### 1. Smooth, plied construction

Look for yarns that are **smooth and evenly spun** with at least 2 plies (the individual strands twisted together). Smooth yarn lets you see your stitches clearly, which is essential when you're learning to read your knitting and spot mistakes.

**Avoid for now:** Fuzzy yarns (mohair, brushed alpaca), bouclé, eyelash yarn, or single-ply yarns. They're gorgeous but they hide your stitches and are tricky to rip back.

### 2. Light or medium colour

Choose a **light to medium colour** — think soft blue, sage green, dusty rose, oatmeal. Light colours let you see the stitch definition clearly. Save those dramatic blacks and deep navies for later — they make it nearly impossible to see what you're doing, especially in less-than-perfect lighting.

### 3. Worsted weight (medium weight)

Yarn comes in different thicknesses called "weights." For beginners, **worsted weight** (also labelled as "medium" or "4" on the ball band) hits the sweet spot. It's thick enough to see and handle easily, but not so bulky that it feels clumsy.

### 4. Forgiving fibre

Some fibres spring back when you knit and unknit them; others hold every crease. For learning, you want something that **bounces back** and doesn't split easily.

## The Best Yarn Fibres for Beginners

### Acrylic — The Practical Choice

**Why it works:** Affordable, widely available, machine-washable, comes in every colour imaginable. Acrylic yarn is forgiving — you can rip it back and re-knit without the yarn looking worse for wear.

**The catch:** It doesn't have the "hand" (the feel) of natural fibres. Some knitters find it squeaky on the needles. But for your first scarf or dishcloth? It's perfect.

**Top picks:**
- **Caron Simply Soft** — Smooth, affordable, beautiful drape. A classic beginner yarn.
- **Red Heart Super Saver** — Extremely budget-friendly and available everywhere. Stiffer than Simply Soft, but practically indestructible.
- **Paintbox Yarns Simply Aran** — Smooth, well-priced, and available in an enormous colour range.

### Wool Blends — The Best of Both Worlds

**Why it works:** Superwash wool blends (usually wool mixed with acrylic or nylon) give you the warmth and elasticity of wool with the easy care of synthetic fibre. Wool's natural springiness makes it a joy to knit with — stitches stay even and the fabric feels alive.

**The catch:** Slightly more expensive than pure acrylic.

**Top picks:**
- **Cascade 220 Superwash** — A workhorse worsted wool that's been a knitter favourite for decades. Great stitch definition, wide colour range.
- **KnitPicks Swish Worsted** — 100% superwash merino at an incredible price point. Soft, smooth, and easy to work with.
- **Berroco Vintage** — A wool/acrylic/nylon blend that's soft, affordable, and available in gorgeous heathered colours.

### Cotton — For Warm-Weather Projects

**Why it works:** If you're starting with a dishcloth or a summer project, cotton is a natural choice. It's sturdy, smooth, and the finished fabric is crisp and satisfying.

**The catch:** Cotton has no stretch, so it can feel less forgiving on the needles than wool. Your tension may be uneven at first — that's normal.

**Top picks:**
- **Lily Sugar'n Cream** — The quintessential dishcloth cotton. Cheap, cheerful, and available at every craft store.
- **Drops Paris** — A beautifully smooth aran-weight cotton with a slightly softer hand than Sugar'n Cream.

## What to Avoid as a Beginner

Not all yarn is beginner-friendly, and some will actively make learning harder:

- **Dark colours** — You can't see your stitches. Save the black yarn for later.
- **Highly textured yarns** — Bouclé, eyelash, thick-and-thin, and novelty yarns look fun on the shelf but are nightmares to learn with.
- **Mohair and brushed fibres** — Impossible to rip back without a tangled mess.
- **Laceweight or fingering weight** — Too thin. You'll spend all your time squinting.
- **Super bulky** — Counterintuitively, very thick yarn can be harder to control for beginners. Stick with worsted or aran weight.
- **Luxury fibres (cashmere, silk, qiviut)** — Beautiful, but expensive. Don't practice on yarn that costs $30 a skein. You want to feel free to make mistakes.

## How Much Yarn Do You Need?

For your first project, buy **one or two skeins** — that's enough for a scarf, a dishcloth, or a generous practice swatch. Most worsted-weight skeins come in 200–220 yard put-ups, which is plenty to get started.

**Pro tip:** Buy an extra skein in the same dye lot. Nothing is more frustrating than running out and discovering your colour is discontinued.

## Where to Buy

- **Local yarn shop (LYS)** — The best option if you have one nearby. Staff can help you choose and you can feel the yarn before buying. Supporting local shops matters.
- **Craft stores** (Joann, Michaels, Hobby Lobby) — Great for affordable acrylic and cotton basics.
- **Online** — KnitPicks, WEBS/yarn.com, LoveCrafts, and Wool Warehouse offer huge selections with detailed descriptions and reviews.

## My Personal Recommendation

If I could only recommend one yarn for a brand-new knitter, it would be **Cascade 220 Superwash in a medium colour**. It's smooth, springy, shows stitch definition beautifully, comes in an absurd number of colours, and it's affordable enough that you won't stress about ripping back. Plus, once you finish your first project, you'll have made something in real wool that you'll actually want to keep.

Your second choice: **KnitPicks Swish Worsted** — same qualities, slightly softer, slightly cheaper.
