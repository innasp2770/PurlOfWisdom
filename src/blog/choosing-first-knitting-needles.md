# How to Choose Your First Knitting Needles

*By Inna | Purl of Wisdom*

**Target keyword:** how to choose knitting needles
**Secondary keywords:** best knitting needles for beginners, what knitting needles should I buy, knitting needle guide, types of knitting needles

---

You've got your yarn. Now you need something to knit it with. But one look at the knitting needle aisle — or a single search online — and suddenly you're staring at bamboo, metal, circular, straight, interchangeable, DPNs, and sizes that go from 0 to 50. What do you actually need?

Let's cut through the noise. Here's everything you need to know to choose your first pair of knitting needles — and nothing you don't.

## The Short Answer

If you want the simplest possible recommendation: **get a pair of US 8 (5 mm) bamboo straight needles, 10 inches long.** They'll work with worsted-weight yarn, they're affordable, and they're comfortable to learn with. You can always expand your collection later.

Now, if you want to understand *why* — read on.

## Needle Types: What's the Difference?

### Straight Needles

The classic. Two sticks with a point on one end and a stopper on the other. This is what most people picture when they think "knitting needles."

**Best for:** Flat knitting (scarves, dishcloths, blanket squares). Simple, intuitive, no confusion about which end to knit from.

**Limitations:** You can only knit flat pieces. For hats, socks, and sweaters knit in the round, you'll eventually need circular or double-pointed needles.

**Recommended length:** 10" for beginners. The standard 14" straight needles can feel unwieldy — the shorter ones are easier to manage and don't poke into the arm of your chair.

### Circular Needles

Two short needle tips connected by a flexible cable. Despite the name, you can knit both flat and in the round with circulars.

**Best for:** Everything. Seriously — many experienced knitters use circulars exclusively. The cable holds the weight of your project, which is easier on your wrists.

**Why a beginner might start here:** If you suspect you'll stick with knitting (and you will), starting with circulars means you won't need to rebuy needles when you graduate to hats and sweaters.

**Recommended cable length:** 24" or 32" for a versatile first pair.

### Double-Pointed Needles (DPNs)

Sets of 4 or 5 short needles, pointed on both ends. Used for knitting small tubes — socks, hat crowns, mittens.

**Best for:** Small-circumference knitting in the round.

**For beginners:** Skip these for now. They're useful but fiddly. You'll know when you need them.

## Needle Materials: What Should They Be Made Of?

This matters more than you'd think. The material affects how the yarn slides (or grips), how the needles feel in your hands, and how much they cost.

### Bamboo / Wood

**Feel:** Warm, lightweight, slightly grippy.

**Why beginners love them:** The gentle grip keeps stitches from sliding off accidentally — a common frustration when you're learning. They're quiet, comfortable, and pleasant to hold for long stretches.

**The catch:** They can bend or break if you knit very tightly or sit on them. The tips aren't as sharp as metal, which can make it harder to get into tight stitches.

**Best picks:**
- **Clover Takumi** — Smooth bamboo, widely available, affordable. The gold standard for beginner bamboo needles.
- **ChiaoGoo Bamboo** — Slightly more polished feel, excellent quality.

### Metal (Aluminium, Stainless Steel, Nickel-Plated)

**Feel:** Cool, smooth, slippery, sturdy.

**Why some knitters prefer them:** Stitches glide effortlessly, which means faster knitting once you've got the basics down. Extremely durable — a good metal needle lasts decades.

**The catch:** Stitches can slide *too* easily when you're learning, leading to dropped stitches. They're also clickier and colder in the hand.

**Best picks:**
- **ChiaoGoo Red Lace** (circulars) — Stainless steel with smooth joins. A favourite among experienced knitters, but excellent quality if you start here.
- **Addi Turbo** — Nickel-plated brass. Fast, smooth, and nearly indestructible.
- **KnitPicks Nickel-Plated** — Great quality at a lower price point.

### Plastic / Acrylic

**Feel:** Lightweight, warm, slightly flexible.

**Why they exist:** Very cheap and available in large sizes (US 13+) for bulky yarn projects.

**For beginners:** Fine in a pinch, but bamboo or metal will give you a better experience. Plastic needles can feel cheap and bend under tension.

## Needle Sizes: What Do the Numbers Mean?

Needle size refers to the **diameter** of the needle. Bigger needles make bigger stitches; smaller needles make smaller stitches.

Every yarn label includes a recommended needle size. For worsted-weight yarn (the best beginner weight), you'll typically want:

| System | Size |
|---|---|
| **US** | 7, 8, or 9 |
| **Metric** | 4.5 mm, 5 mm, or 5.5 mm |
| **UK** | 7, 6, or 5 (confusingly, UK sizes go in the opposite direction) |

**Start with US 8 (5 mm).** It's the most common pairing for worsted-weight yarn and gives a balanced fabric — not too tight, not too loose.

**Important:** Always check your yarn label for the recommended needle size. It's printed right there, usually with a little needle icon.

## What About Interchangeable Sets?

Interchangeable needle sets give you multiple tip sizes that screw onto different cable lengths. They're the Swiss Army knife of knitting needles.

**Should a beginner buy one?** Not yet. They're an investment ($60–$200+) and you don't know your preferences yet. Once you've knit a few projects and know whether you prefer bamboo or metal, circulars or straights, *then* invest in a set that matches your style.

**When you're ready, the most popular sets are:**
- ChiaoGoo Twist (metal)
- Knitter's Pride Dreamz (wood)
- KnitPicks Options (nickel or wood)
- Addi Click (metal)

## Common Beginner Questions

**Q: Can I use any size needle with any yarn?**
Technically yes, but your fabric will look and feel very different. Using the yarn's recommended needle size gives you a balanced fabric. Using larger needles makes a looser, drapier fabric; smaller needles make a tighter, denser one.

**Q: Does needle brand really matter?**
At the beginner level, not much. Any bamboo or metal needle from a reputable brand will serve you well. The differences become more noticeable as you knit more and develop preferences.

**Q: I'm left-handed. Do I need special needles?**
No. Knitting needles are symmetrical. Some left-handed knitters mirror the standard technique, others learn the standard way — either works, and neither requires different needles.

**Q: How many needles do I need to start?**
One pair of straight needles (that's two needles) or one circular needle. That's it.
