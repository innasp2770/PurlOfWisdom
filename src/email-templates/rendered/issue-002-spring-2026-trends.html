Spring 2026 Knitting Trends — What's on the Needles

Every season brings new energy to the knitting world. Here's what we're seeing across Ravelry, indie dyer shops, and our own project bags this March.

  ✦ ✦ ✦  
1

Lace Weight Is Back

For years, chunky and super-bulky ruled. Knitters wanted quick wins — a hat in an evening, a blanket in a weekend. That pendulum is swinging.

Lace-weight shawls are showing up everywhere. The appeal makes sense: spring weather calls for something lighter, and there's a quiet satisfaction in watching delicate fabric grow row by row. If you've never worked with lace weight, start with a simple crescent shawl in stockinette with a garter border. You'll be surprised how quickly you adjust to the thinner yarn.

Try this: Malabrigo Lace in "Polar Morn" — a cool blue-grey that practically glows in natural light.

2

Butter Yellow Everything

The colour of the season is unmistakable. Butter yellow — soft, warm, not quite gold — is dominating yarn shop shelves and Instagram project pages alike. It pairs beautifully with denim, works as a neutral for spring layering, and looks stunning in cotton or linen blends.

We're seeing it in cropped tees, market bags, and lightweight cowls. If a full garment feels like too much commitment, a pair of butter-yellow ankle socks is the perfect entry point.

Our pick: Quince & Co. Sparrow in "Honey" — a linen-blend that knits up with gorgeous drape.

3

Modular Knitting

Mitered squares, hexagons, entrelac — modular construction is having a real moment. The beauty of modular knitting is that each unit is a complete mini-project. You get the dopamine hit of finishing something without committing to an entire garment.

It's also wildly practical for using up stash. Each module can be a different colour or yarn weight, making this the ultimate scrap-busting technique.

Starting point: Try a simple mitered square dishcloth. Cast on 47 stitches, decrease at the centre every other row. You'll have the technique down in one evening.

 

✍️ Technique of the Week

Reading Your Knitting

This might be the single most valuable skill a knitter can develop. Reading your knitting means looking at the fabric on your needles and understanding what happened in the rows below — without checking your pattern.

• Knit vs. purl: Knit stitches have a smooth V on the face. Purls have a bump. Once you can spot these instantly, you'll catch mistakes before they become problems.
• Counting rows: Each V in stockinette is one row. In garter stitch, each ridge is two rows.
• Spotting a dropped stitch: Look for a horizontal ladder running down between stitches. The sooner you catch it, the easier the fix.

Spend five minutes at the start of your next session just looking at your work. Don't knit — just read. It changes everything.
