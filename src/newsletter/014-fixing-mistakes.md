## The mistake everyone makes about mistakes

New knitters treat errors like emergencies. A dropped stitch triggers panic. A miscrossed cable means the whole thing is ruined. An extra stitch that appeared from nowhere? Must be a sign that knitting isn't for you.

Experienced knitters know the truth: mistakes are normal, constant, and completely fixable. The difference between a beginner and a confident knitter isn't the number of mistakes they make. It's how quickly they spot them and how calmly they fix them.

This issue is about building that skill. Reading your knitting, catching problems early, and — when needed — ripping back without drama.

## Learning to read your knitting

Before you can fix mistakes, you need to see them. That means learning to read your knitting — understanding what each stitch looks like and what it's supposed to look like.

**Knit stitches** look like small V shapes stacked on top of each other. The right leg of the V sits in front of the left leg. If the V is twisted (left leg in front), the stitch was worked through the back loop, either intentionally or by accident.

**Purl stitches** look like bumps or horizontal bars. On the wrong side of stockinette, every row is purls. If you're working in the round and see a bump on the right side, something went wrong.

**Yarn overs** create deliberate holes with a visible eyelet. If you see a hole you didn't plan, you've got an accidental yarn over — usually from bringing the yarn to the wrong side between stitches.

**Dropped stitches** look like ladders running down the fabric. The stitch has slipped off the needle and is unravelling row by row. The sooner you catch it, the fewer rows you need to fix.

Get in the habit of scanning your work every few rows. Not obsessively — just a quick glance at the fabric. Does it look right? Are the stitch counts correct? Does the pattern repeat line up? This five-second check saves hours of ripping later.

## The five most common mistakes (and how to fix them)

### 1. Dropped stitch

A stitch falls off the needle and starts to ladder. Don't panic — it won't unravel to nothing overnight.

**Fix:** Insert a crochet hook from front to back through the dropped stitch. Catch the lowest horizontal ladder bar and pull it through the stitch. Repeat for each ladder rung until you're back at the current row. Slip the stitch back onto your left needle, making sure it's not twisted.

For purl-side pickups, insert the hook from back to front instead.

### 2. Extra stitch

Your stitch count is higher than it should be. Usually caused by an accidental yarn over or by knitting into the bar between stitches.

**Fix:** If you catch it in the same row, simply knit or purl two stitches together at the point where the extra appeared. If you're unsure where it came from, work to the end of the row, count carefully, and decrease where the fabric looks cleanest.

### 3. Twisted stitch

The stitch sits on the needle with the leading leg in the back instead of the front. Knitting it normally will produce a twisted, tighter stitch.

**Fix:** If you catch it before working it, simply slip the stitch off, turn it around, and place it back with the leading leg in front. If you've already knit several rows past it, decide whether it bothers you. In most fabrics, a single twisted stitch is invisible.

### 4. Split yarn

Your needle went through the middle of the yarn instead of the whole strand. The stitch looks thin and messy.

**Fix:** Slip back to the split stitch and rework it, making sure the needle catches all the plies. If you've gone past it, a single split stitch usually blocks out and disappears in the finished fabric. Only fix it if you can see it from arm's length.

### 5. Wrong stitch (knit instead of purl, or vice versa)

You knit where you should have purled, or the other way around.

**Fix:** If it's one or two rows back, you can drop just that stitch down to the error row and pick it up correctly with a crochet hook. If it's further back, you'll need to decide whether to live with it or rip back. In textured patterns like seed stitch, a single wrong stitch blends in. In cables or lace, it's more visible.

## The art of frogging

"Frogging" means ripping out your knitting — rip it, rip it (ribbit). It sounds dramatic, but it's one of the most useful skills you can develop.

**When to frog:**
- The mistake is structural (wrong size, wrong gauge, misread pattern)
- The error is visible and it will bother you every time you wear the thing
- You're unhappy with the yarn or colour choice
- The project has gone off the rails and starting over is faster than fixing

**When not to frog:**
- The mistake is only visible if you point it out
- You're more than halfway done and the error is minor
- You're tired and emotional (sleep on it first)
- The "mistake" is actually just a design feature you didn't plan

### How to frog safely

1. **Mark your stopping point.** If you're ripping back to a specific row, thread a lifeline through that row first — a length of smooth waste yarn on a tapestry needle, woven through every stitch. When you rip down to the lifeline, your stitches are caught and safe.

2. **Remove the needles.** Take your knitting off the needles entirely. Trying to rip back stitch by stitch is slow and increases the chance of dropping stitches.

3. **Pull gently.** Tug the working yarn to unravel. Go slowly near the stopping point so you don't rip past it.

4. **Pick up stitches carefully.** Using a needle one or two sizes smaller than your working needle, pick up each stitch from the lifeline or the live row. Check that no stitches are twisted. Once they're all on the needle, switch back to your working needle and continue.

5. **Wind the ripped yarn.** Don't just stuff it back in the bag. Wind it into a ball or use a niddy-noddy to re-skein it. Kinked yarn from ripping knits unevenly — give it a light steam or a soak to relax the kinks before reusing.

## Lifelines: your safety net

If you're working a complex pattern — lace, cables, colourwork — place lifelines every ten to twenty rows. Thread waste yarn through every stitch on the needle using a tapestry needle. If you need to rip back, you'll never go further than the nearest lifeline.

Some circular needles have a small hole in the join specifically for threading lifelines. If yours does, use it. It's the best feature nobody talks about.

Label your lifelines with the row number on a small piece of tape. When you're 200 rows into a lace shawl, you won't remember which row you threaded the lifeline through.

## The intentional frog

Here's a mindset shift that changes everything: frogging isn't failure. It's editing.

Writers delete paragraphs. Painters scrape canvas. Musicians re-record takes. Knitters frog. It's the same creative process — making, evaluating, and remaking until the thing is right.

Some of the best knitters frog constantly. Not because they make more mistakes, but because they have higher standards and the confidence to start over. They know the yarn doesn't care how many times it's been knit up. The stitches don't remember.

If you've been avoiding frogging because it feels like wasted time, flip it: every row you knit is practice. Even the rows you rip out taught your hands something. The second time through is always faster, always better, always more confident.

## This week's exercise

Pick up a current project and spend five minutes reading your knitting. Look at the last ten rows. Can you identify every stitch type? Can you spot where you changed tension, where you started a new row, where you might have pulled a stitch too tight?

If you're feeling brave, practise a deliberate frog. Cast on twenty stitches, knit ten rows, thread a lifeline through row five, then rip back to it. Pick up the stitches and keep going. Do this once and the fear disappears.
