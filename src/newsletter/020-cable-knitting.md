## The core mechanic

Every cable, from a simple two-stitch twist to an elaborate Aran panel, works the same way: you take a group of stitches off the needle, hold them aside, knit the next group, then knit the held stitches. By changing which group gets knitted first, you change the direction the cable crosses.

That's the entire technique. Everything else — width, frequency, direction — is just variation on this single move.

## Left cross vs right cross

**Right-crossing cable (C4B, C6B, etc.):** Slip stitches to a cable needle and hold them to the *back* of the work. Knit the next stitches from the left needle, then knit the stitches from the cable needle. The cable leans to the right.

**Left-crossing cable (C4F, C6F, etc.):** Slip stitches to a cable needle and hold them to the *front*. Knit the next stitches, then the held ones. The cable leans to the left.

The number tells you the total stitches involved. C6B means six stitches total — slip three to the back, knit three, knit the three from the cable needle.

**Memory trick:** B = Back = Right. F = Front = Left. (The held stitches travel to the back-right or front-left of the work.)

## Reading cable charts

Cable charts use symbols that look like the cables themselves — a diagonal line or braid crossing left or right over a background of dots (purl) or blanks (knit).

**Key conventions:**
- A right-leaning cross symbol means hold to back (right cross).
- A left-leaning cross symbol means hold to front (left cross).
- The number of squares the symbol spans tells you how many stitches are in the cross.
- Purl stitches (dots or dashes) typically form the background. Cables almost always sit on a reverse stockinette (purl) background — the purled fabric recedes, making the cable pop forward.

**Reading tip:** Before you start, look at the full chart and identify the repeating pattern. Most cables cross every 4, 6, or 8 rows. Mark the crossing rows with a highlighter. On non-crossing rows, you're just knitting the knits and purling the purls — straightforward stockinette columns.

## The cable needle question

A cable needle is a short, bent or hooked needle that holds stitches while you cross a cable. They work. They're also slow, fiddly, and easy to lose in the sofa cushions.

**Cabling without a cable needle:** For cables up to about 8 stitches wide, you can skip the cable needle entirely.

The method:
1. Identify the stitches that need to cross.
2. Slip all the cable stitches off the left needle. (Yes, they're now free. Don't panic.)
3. Pinch the back group with your fingers while you slide the left needle into the front group.
4. Slip the back group onto the left needle.
5. Knit all stitches in the new order.

This sounds terrifying the first time. The stitches won't drop if your tension is reasonable and you work quickly. Try it on a swatch first. Once you get the feel, you'll never go back to a cable needle for small cables.

For wide cables (10+ stitches), a cable needle is still useful. A DPN works fine if you don't have a dedicated cable needle.

## Designing your first cable panel

Cables need a specific setup in your fabric:

**Purl background:** Cable stitches are always knit stitches sitting on a purl background. The contrast is what makes the cable visible. Without the purl frame, cables flatten into the surrounding stockinette and lose their definition.

**Extra width:** Cables pull the fabric in. A six-stitch cable will be narrower than six stitches of stockinette. If you're adding cables to an existing pattern, you'll need extra stitches to compensate. A common rule of thumb: add 1-2 extra stitches per cable repeat.

**Row gauge matters more than usual.** Cables compress the row gauge — the fabric is denser vertically. If you're substituting a cable panel into a pattern, check your row gauge carefully.

## Classic cable patterns to learn

**Simple rope cable (C6B or C6F every 6th row):** Six stitches that cross every six rows, all in the same direction. Creates a rope that spirals continuously. This is the building block of cable knitting.

**Braided cable:** Three sets of stitches that alternate crossing left and right, creating a plaited effect. Typically worked over 9 or 12 stitches. More complex to track but visually dramatic.

**Honeycomb:** Alternating left and right crosses on offset rows. Creates a textured, all-over pattern that looks intricate but uses only two cable rows in the repeat.

**Saxon braid / Celtic plait:** Wide panels of interlocking cables that weave over and under each other. The showpiece of Aran knitting. These look impossibly complex but are just multiple rope cables interacting on a shared grid.

**Twisted stitches (mini cables):** Two-stitch crosses worked without a cable needle. These are quick, subtle, and add beautiful texture to ribbing, sock cuffs, and hat brims. Sometimes noted as RT (right twist) and LT (left twist) rather than C2B/C2F.

## Your first cable project

**Option 1 — A cable sampler scarf.** Cast on enough for a central cable panel (6-8 stitches) with purl borders (3-4 stitches each side). Work a simple rope cable for 15cm, switch to a braided cable, then a honeycomb section. You'll learn three patterns in one project, and the scarf will look intentionally designed.

**Option 2 — A cabled headband or ear warmer.** Cast on 20 stitches, work a central 8-stitch cable on a purl ground. Quick to finish, immediately wearable, and the narrow width means you're never juggling too many stitches.

**Option 3 — A cable-edged dishcloth.** Garter stitch body with a four-stitch twisted rib cable running up each side. Low stakes, fast, and teaches you to maintain cable rhythm alongside plain knitting.

## Common mistakes

**Crossing in the wrong direction.** If your cable leans left when it should lean right (or vice versa), you held the stitches to the wrong side. Check: Back = Right, Front = Left.

**Forgetting to cross.** If you miss a crossing row, the cable just stops — you get a section of plain stockinette columns instead of a twist. Use a row counter. Mark crossing rows on your chart. Some knitters place a stitch marker before and after each cable panel to stay oriented.

**Tight cables.** Cables naturally pull the fabric tighter, but if your cable is uncomfortably stiff, you may be knitting the held stitches too tightly when you pick them back up. Relax the tension on the first stitch after the cross.

**Tangled cable needle.** If the cable needle keeps sliding out or getting in the way, try a bent or U-shaped cable needle, or switch to cabling without one. A straight DPN will spin and slide; the bend is what keeps the stitches secure.

**Losing your place in a complex cable panel.** Read the cable below before you work the next row. If the last cross went right, and the chart says the next cross goes left, you should be able to see that in the fabric. Learning to read your cables (not just the chart) is the skill that makes complex panels manageable.

## Cables beyond scarves

Once you're comfortable with basic cables:

- **Aran sweaters.** The classic application — multiple cable panels running vertically, separated by purl columns. Each panel is a different pattern. Traditional Aran sweaters are a sampler of cable techniques.
- **Cable and lace combinations.** Cables with yarn overs between the twists create open, lighter fabric. Beautiful in shawls.
- **Travelling stitches.** Cables that move diagonally across the fabric rather than running straight up. The technique behind Bavarian twisted stitch patterns and many modern shawl designs.
- **Reversible cables.** Special constructions (often using 1x1 ribbing within the cable) that look good on both sides. Ideal for scarves and blankets.
