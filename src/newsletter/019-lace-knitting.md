## The secret of lace

Every piece of knitted lace — from a delicate wedding shawl to a simple eyelet border — is built from exactly two elements: yarn overs (holes) and decreases (which lean left or right to shape the fabric around those holes).

That's it. Yarn overs create the open spaces. Decreases consume the extra stitches the yarn overs added, keeping your stitch count stable. The specific arrangement of these two moves is what creates every lace pattern ever designed.

Once you internalise this, lace stops being mysterious and starts being logical.

## Yarn overs

A yarn over is the simplest stitch increase in knitting. You wrap the yarn around the needle without knitting into an existing stitch, creating a new stitch and — crucially — a decorative hole on the next row.

**Between two knit stitches:** Bring the yarn to the front (as if to purl), then knit the next stitch. The yarn travels over the top of the needle, creating the extra loop.

**Between a purl and a knit:** The yarn is already in front. Just knit the next stitch — the yarn wraps over the needle automatically.

**Between a knit and a purl:** Bring the yarn to the front, wrap it over the needle to the back, then bring it to the front again to purl.

If your yarn over isn't creating a visible hole, you're probably twisting it on the return row. When you encounter a yarn over on the wrong side, work into the front leg. If you knit into the back leg, it closes the hole.

## The paired decreases

Every yarn over needs a decrease partner to keep the stitch count even. The three decreases you'll use in lace:

**K2tog (knit two together):** Knit two stitches as one. The resulting stitch leans right. Use this to the right of a yarn over for a symmetrical effect.

**SSK (slip, slip, knit):** Slip two stitches knitwise one at a time, then knit them together through the back loops. Leans left. Use this to the left of a yarn over.

**S2KP (slip 2, knit 1, pass slipped stitches over):** A centred double decrease. Removes two stitches at once and sits on a vertical axis. Used in many leaf and diamond motifs where the central stitch should stack neatly.

The direction of your decreases matters. When the decreases lean toward the yarn overs, the pattern flows smoothly. When they lean away, you get a different visual texture. Neither is wrong — but lace charts assume you know which decrease to use where.

## Reading lace charts

Lace patterns are almost always charted rather than written out. A written instruction for a complex lace row might look like "K2, YO, SSK, K3, K2tog, YO, K1, YO, SSK, K3, K2tog, YO, K2" — and that's a simple one. A chart shows the same information as a visual grid where you can actually see the pattern forming.

**Key conventions:**
- Each square represents one stitch.
- Right-side rows read right to left (the direction you knit them). Wrong-side rows read left to right.
- A circle or "O" means yarn over.
- A right-leaning line means K2tog. A left-leaning line means SSK.
- An empty square usually means knit on RS, purl on WS.
- Shaded or "no stitch" squares are placeholders — skip them. They keep the chart aligned when stitch counts change.

**The cheat:** If you're new to charts, photocopy the chart and use a sticky note to cover the rows above where you're working. Move it up as you finish each row. You'll see the pattern emerge below the sticky note, which helps you catch mistakes early.

Many knitters who insist they "can't read charts" simply haven't tried with a lace pattern. Lace is where charts become essential, and also where they start making intuitive sense — because you can see the diagonals and motifs in the grid before you even pick up your needles.

## Choosing lace yarn

Lace patterns work at any weight, but lighter yarn creates more dramatic results.

**Lace weight (1-ply or 2-ply):** The classic choice for shawls and stoles. Gossamer-thin, often 800-1400 metres per 100g. Knit on 3.25-4mm needles for an open, airy fabric. Not for beginners — mistakes are harder to spot and fix.

**Fingering weight (4-ply):** The practical sweet spot. Open enough to show lace patterns clearly, sturdy enough to handle ripping back. Most lace shawl patterns are written for fingering weight.

**DK or worsted:** Produces chunkier lace — more like a textured stitch pattern than traditional lace. Good for learning because the stitches are large and easy to read.

**Fibre:** Smooth yarns show lace patterns best. Merino, silk, silk-merino blends, alpaca-silk, BFL (Bluefaced Leicester). Avoid fuzzy yarns like mohair for your first lace project — the halo obscures the stitch definition. (Later, when you're confident, a mohair-silk held with a lace-weight yarn creates stunning halos around the openwork.)

**Colour:** Solid or semi-solid colours. Variegated yarn fights with lace patterns — you can't see the holes clearly when the colour is constantly changing.

## Your first lace project

Don't start with a full shawl. Start with something flat and small:

**Option 1 — A lace sampler scarf.** Cast on 25-30 stitches in fingering or DK weight. Work a different simple lace pattern every 10cm: an eyelet row, a chevron, a feather and fan, a simple leaf. You'll learn how each pattern works without committing to hundreds of rows of the same motif.

**Option 2 — A feather and fan scarf.** The "Old Shale" or "feather and fan" pattern is the gateway drug of lace knitting. It uses only knit, purl, K2tog, and yarn over — no SSK required. The wave pattern is forgiving (minor tension variations disappear in the ripple), and the four-row repeat is easy to memorise.

The basic repeat:
- **Row 1:** Knit.
- **Row 2:** Purl.
- **Row 3:** *(K2tog) three times, (YO, K1) six times, (K2tog) three times.* Repeat across.
- **Row 4:** Knit (or purl — both work; knitting creates a garter ridge between the waves).

That's the entire pattern. It produces beautiful scalloped waves with zero chart reading required.

**Option 3 — A dishcloth with an eyelet border.** Cast on, work stockinette with a garter border, and add a simple eyelet row (K2, *YO, K2tog*, repeat) every 10 rows. Low stakes, quick to finish, and you practise the core yarn-over-plus-decrease mechanic.

## Common mistakes and fixes

**Losing or gaining stitches.** Count after every right-side row until you trust the pattern. Every yarn over should have a corresponding decrease. If your count is off, you missed one or the other.

**Forgetting a yarn over.** If you catch it on the next row, you can lift the bar between stitches and work it as a yarn over — it won't be as open, but it's better than ripping back. If you catch it several rows later, use a crochet hook to ladder up and create the missing yarn over.

**Twisted yarn overs.** The hole should be open and round. If it's closed or barely visible, you're working into the back leg on the wrong side. Wrap consistently and always knit into the front leg of the yarn over on the return row.

**Getting lost in the chart.** Use a row counter religiously. Mark your place on the chart. If you put the knitting down mid-row, leave a note (literally — a sticky note on the work) saying which row and which stitch you stopped at.

**Ripping back lace.** Thread a lifeline first. A lifeline is a length of smooth, contrasting waste yarn threaded through every stitch on the needle at a known-good row. If you need to rip back, you rip to the lifeline and pick up the held stitches. Without a lifeline, ripping back lace is a nightmare of dropped yarn overs and tangled decreases.

**Lifeline tip:** Many circular needles have a small hole in the join for exactly this purpose — you can thread dental floss or fine yarn through the hole, and it lays a lifeline automatically as you knit the row.

## Blocking makes lace

Lace fabric looks like a crumpled, unpromising mess straight off the needles. This is normal. Lace must be blocked to reach its final form.

Soak the finished piece in cool water with a drop of wool wash for 20 minutes. Squeeze gently (never wring). Roll in a towel to remove excess water. Pin it out to the finished dimensions on blocking mats, stretching the fabric open so the yarn overs bloom and the pattern becomes visible.

The transformation is dramatic. Pre-blocking, your shawl looks like a dishrag. Post-blocking, it looks like lace. This is not an exaggeration — if you've never blocked lace before, the before-and-after will feel like a magic trick.

Use rust-proof T-pins or blocking wires for straight edges. For scalloped edges, pin each point individually.

## Moving beyond basics

Once you're comfortable with simple lace:

- **Try a triangular shawl.** Most start with a few stitches and increase every other row, adding lace patterning as the shawl grows. The increases are built into the construction.
- **Combine lace with other techniques.** Lace borders on stockinette sweaters. Lace panels in otherwise plain scarves. Lace yokes on top-down sweaters.
- **Explore Estonian or Shetland lace traditions.** Nupps (tiny bobbles created with multiple yarn overs), elaborate edgings, and shawl constructions refined over centuries.
- **Try charting your own pattern.** Once you understand how yarn overs and decreases interact, you can sketch patterns on graph paper. Start simple — a small leaf or a diamond — and swatch it.
