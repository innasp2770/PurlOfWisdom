## The case for tubes

Flat knitting has its place. But once you discover knitting in the round, a lot of things get simpler. No purling on the wrong side — stockinette is just knit, knit, knit. No seams to sew at the end. No selvedge edges to fuss over. The right side always faces you, so you can see your pattern developing in real time.

Hats, socks, mittens, cowls, sleeves, and entire sweaters can all be worked in the round. It's not an advanced technique. It's a different approach, and most knitters find it faster and more enjoyable once they get past the first awkward round.

## Three tools for the job

### Circular needles

Two needle tips connected by a flexible cable. This is the most common tool for knitting in the round and the one most knitters reach for first.

**When to use them:** Any project where the circumference roughly matches the cable length. A 40 cm circular works for hats. A 60 cm or 80 cm works for sweater bodies. A 100 cm or 120 cm works for large blankets knit flat (circulars aren't just for tubes — they hold a lot of stitches).

**The key rule:** Your stitch count must fill the cable comfortably. If there are too few stitches, they'll stretch across the cable and the join won't work. If you have 80 stitches on a 100 cm cable, the stitches can't reach from one tip to the other. Use a shorter cable or switch to magic loop.

**Tips:**
- Buy interchangeable sets. You get multiple tip sizes and cable lengths in one case. Far more cost-effective and flexible than fixed circulars.
- Cable memory (kinking) is real in cheap cables. Soak them in hot water for a minute to relax the cable before knitting.
- The join is everything. A smooth join between tip and cable means your stitches glide. A rough join catches yarn every stitch.

### Double-pointed needles (DPNs)

Short needles, usually sold in sets of four or five. You distribute stitches across three or four needles and knit with the free one.

**When to use them:** Small circumferences where circular needles won't work — sock toes, mitten tips, the crown of a hat, i-cord. Anywhere you have fewer than about 40 stitches in the round.

**The reputation:** DPNs look intimidating. Four or five needles poking out in every direction like a hedgehog. But the reality is you're only ever working with two needles at a time — the one in your right hand and the one you're knitting off of. The others are just holding stitches.

**Tips:**
- Use five needles (stitches on four, knit with the fifth) for an even distribution. Four needles (stitches on three) work too but can create visible "ladders" at the transitions.
- Wood or bamboo DPNs are less slippery than metal, which helps when you're learning. Stitches are less likely to slide off.
- Point protectors on the non-working ends prevent stitches from escaping when you put the project down.

### Magic loop

One long circular needle (at least 80 cm, ideally 100 cm) used to knit any circumference, no matter how small. You pull a loop of cable out between the stitches, dividing them into two halves, and knit each half in turn.

**When to use it:** Whenever you'd use DPNs but prefer not to. Socks, mittens, sleeves, anything small. Also useful for knitting two items at once on the same needle — two socks, two sleeves — so they match in length.

**How it works:**
1. Cast on. Slide the stitches to the cable.
2. Divide them roughly in half. Pull a loop of cable out between the two halves.
3. Slide the front half to the tip. Let the back half rest on the cable.
4. Knit the front stitches. When you reach the end, turn the work.
5. Pull the cable loop, slide the new front half to the tip, knit across.
6. Repeat.

**Tips:**
- The cable loop always comes out at the same two points. If you notice laddering there, tug the first stitch of each half a bit tighter.
- Use a cable that's flexible enough to bend sharply at the loop point. Stiff cables fight you.
- Magic loop is slower than short circulars for large projects. It's best for small-circumference work.

## Joining in the round

This is where most beginners get tripped up. You've cast on your stitches, arranged them on your needles, and now you need to connect the first stitch to the last without twisting.

**Step by step:**
1. Cast on one extra stitch.
2. Arrange stitches on your needle(s). Lay everything flat on a table and make sure the cast-on edge runs along the inside — not spiralling over any needle.
3. Place a stitch marker to mark the beginning of the round.
4. Slip the extra stitch from the right needle to the left needle.
5. Knit the first two stitches on the left needle together (the slipped stitch and the original first stitch). This closes the gap at the join.
6. Knit your first round.

**The twist check:** Before joining, spread your stitches along the needles and visually check that the cast-on edge doesn't wrap around a needle. If the bottom edge goes over the top of any needle, you have a twist. Fix it before knitting — a twist is permanent and unfixable without ripping out.

Some knitters skip the extra-stitch method and simply knit the first stitch tightly, then use the tail to close any gap when weaving in ends. Both approaches work. Use whichever feels cleaner.

## Managing the beginning of round

You need to know where each round starts. A stitch marker on the needle is the standard approach. Move it from left to right needle each time you reach it.

For colourwork, you may need a second marker to track pattern repeats. Use different-coloured markers for different purposes.

In flat knitting, you count rows by looking at the edges. In the round, there's no edge. Keep a row counter on your needle or tally rounds in a notebook. For longer projects, this saves you from counting hundreds of rows later.

## The ladder problem

"Laddering" is the loose column of stitches that sometimes appears where you transition between DPNs or at the magic loop divide points. The cause: the first stitch after a needle change tends to be looser because there's a gap between the needles.

**Fixes:**
- Knit the first two stitches on each new needle tightly.
- Shift the transition point every few rounds by knitting one or two extra stitches past the end of the needle before switching.
- Use five DPNs instead of four to reduce the angle at each corner.
- For magic loop, keep the cable loop tight and tug the first stitch firmly.

## Flat vs. in the round: what changes

If you're converting a flat pattern to in-the-round (or vice versa), a few things shift:

- **Stockinette:** Flat = knit RS, purl WS. In the round = knit every round.
- **Garter stitch:** Flat = knit every row. In the round = alternate knit and purl rounds.
- **Wrong-side rows disappear.** If a flat pattern says "purl across" on even rows, you skip those when working in the round.
- **Stitch count changes.** Flat patterns include selvedge stitches. Remove them when converting to round.
- **Decreases and shaping** may need to be mirrored differently since you never work from the wrong side.

Not every pattern converts cleanly. Cables and lace in the round require re-reading the chart without the wrong-side rows. Most modern patterns specify "in the round" or "flat" and shouldn't need converting.

## This week's exercise

If you've never knit in the round, cast on a simple hat. Sixty stitches on a 40 cm circular needle in worsted weight. Knit 2x2 ribbing for 5 cm, then stockinette until it's about 20 cm tall. Decrease for the crown using DPNs or magic loop when the stitches get too few for the circular.

If you already knit in the round but haven't tried magic loop, take a 100 cm circular and knit a small tube — a phone cosy, a mug cosy, anything. Get comfortable with the cable pull and the two-half rhythm. It opens up a lot of possibilities.
