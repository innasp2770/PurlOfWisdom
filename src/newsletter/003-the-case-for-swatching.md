## Let's talk about the thing nobody wants to do

If there's one piece of knitting advice that gets ignored more than any other, it's this: *swatch first.*

We get it. You've got beautiful yarn. You've found the perfect pattern. The last thing you want to do is knit a little square that isn't the thing you actually want to make. It feels like homework before the fun starts.

But here's what we've learned the hard way — and what every experienced knitter will tell you over a quiet cup of tea — swatching isn't busywork. It's insurance.

## Why gauge matters more than you think

Gauge is the number of stitches and rows per inch (or 10cm) that your combination of yarn, needles, and hands produces. Change any one of those variables, and the finished fabric changes.

The pattern says 20 stitches = 4 inches in stockinette on US 7 needles. You pick up your 7s, cast on, and your stitches come out at 22 per 4 inches. That's only two stitches off, right?

Wrong. On a sweater with a 40-inch chest, those two extra stitches per inch add up to a garment that's nearly 4 inches too small. That's the difference between a sweater you wear every day and one that lives in the donation pile.

## How to swatch without hating it

Here's our approach — tested across dozens of projects:

**1. Cast on more than you need.** The pattern says 20 stitches per 4 inches? Cast on 30–36. Edge stitches behave differently than interior ones, so you need enough fabric to measure the middle section accurately.

**2. Knit at least 5 inches.** Rows matter too. Knit enough that you can lay a ruler across the centre of your swatch, well away from the cast-on and bind-off edges.

**3. Wash it.** This is the step most people skip, and it's arguably the most important. Some yarns bloom after washing — they fill out and get softer. Others tighten up. Superwash merino in particular can grow significantly when wet-blocked. Your pre-wash gauge and your post-wash gauge may be completely different numbers.

**4. Measure after it dries.** Lay the swatch flat (no stretching, no pinning). Place your ruler in the middle and count stitches across 4 inches. Then count rows over 4 inches. Write these numbers down.

**5. Adjust your needles, not your expectations.** Too many stitches per inch? Go up a needle size. Too few? Go down. Knit another swatch. Yes, really. Two swatches are still faster than frogging a sleeve.

## The swatch bonus nobody talks about

Here's the thing that converts swatch-skeptics: a swatch is your first real conversation with the yarn.

You learn how it feels in your hands. Whether it splits on your needles. How the colour pools or stripes. Whether the stitch definition is crisp enough for cables or too busy for lace. You discover all of this before you've committed 40 hours to a project.

Think of it as a first date. Low stakes, high information.

## Technique of the Week

### The Long-Tail Swatch Cast-On Trick

When swatching, use a provisional or scrap-yarn cast-on instead of long-tail. Why? Because the long-tail cast-on creates a firm, inelastic edge that can distort your gauge measurement near the bottom of the swatch.

Instead, try this: Cast on with scrap yarn using any method. Knit 4–6 rows in scrap yarn. Switch to your project yarn and start your swatch. When you're done measuring, you can unravel the scrap yarn and reclaim those stitches — or just leave it. The point is that your project-yarn rows aren't fighting against a tight cast-on edge.

## Pattern Pick: The Weekender Swatch Blanket

Here's an idea that turns swatching from a chore into a project: save every swatch you make. When you have enough, seam them together into a patchwork blanket. Each square tells the story of a project — the yarn you chose, the needles you used, the season you knit it in.

It's a knitting diary you can wrap yourself in.
