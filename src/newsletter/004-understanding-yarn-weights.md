## The yarn aisle doesn't have to be intimidating

You walk into a yarn shop. The labels say things like "sport weight," "DK," "worsted," and "aran." Some of them sound like they should mean the same thing. Some of them do, depending on who you ask.

Yarn weight is one of those things that seems more complicated than it actually is. Once you understand the basic system, the whole knitting world opens up — patterns make more sense, substitutions become possible, and you stop buying yarn that fights your project.

Let's break it down.

## The weight spectrum

Yarn weight refers to the thickness of the yarn strand. The Craft Yarn Council standardised a numbering system from 0 to 7, thinnest to thickest. Here's what matters in practice:

**Lace (0) and Fingering (1)** — thin, delicate yarns. Think shawls, lightweight socks, and intricate colourwork. These take patience. A fingering-weight sweater is a commitment measured in months, not weeks. But the drape and detail are worth it.

**Sport (2) and DK (3)** — the sweet spot for many knitters. Thick enough to see progress, thin enough for garments that aren't bulky. DK stands for "double knitting," and it's probably the most versatile weight you can buy. Hats, baby clothes, lighter sweaters, accessories — DK does it all.

**Worsted (4) and Aran (4-5)** — the classic. Most beginner patterns call for worsted weight because it knits up quickly, shows stitch definition clearly, and works with a comfortable needle size (US 7-9). If you're not sure what to buy, worsted is rarely wrong.

**Bulky (5) and Super Bulky (6-7)** — fast and dramatic. A bulky-weight cowl can be done in an evening. These yarns are forgiving for beginners and satisfying for anyone who wants quick results. The trade-off is that finished items are heavier and warmer — great for winter accessories, less ideal for a spring cardigan.

## The label tells you almost everything

Every skein of yarn has a ball band (the label wrapped around it). Here's what to look for:

- **Weight category** — often shown as a small yarn icon with a number (0–7)
- **Recommended needle size** — a good starting point, but your gauge swatch has the final word
- **Gauge** — how many stitches per 4 inches (10 cm) on the recommended needles
- **Fibre content** — wool, cotton, acrylic, blends
- **Yardage and grams** — critical for figuring out how many skeins you need
- **Dye lot** — buy all your skeins from the same dye lot, or alternate skeins every two rows to avoid colour shifts

## Fibre matters as much as weight

Two yarns can both be DK weight and feel completely different. That's the fibre talking.

**Wool** is the workhorse. It's warm, elastic, blocks beautifully, and is forgiving of uneven tension. Superwash wool can go in the washing machine. Non-superwash wool felts if you look at it wrong — but it also has more body and is better for colourwork.

**Cotton** has no stretch. It's cool, crisp, and perfect for dishcloths, summer tops, and anything you want to hold its shape without blocking. But it can be hard on the hands over long sessions, and it shows every uneven stitch.

**Acrylic** gets a bad reputation in some circles, but modern acrylics are soft, affordable, machine-washable, and come in every colour imaginable. For baby items, charity knitting, or projects that need to survive a washing machine, acrylic is practical and smart.

**Blends** give you the best of both worlds. A wool-nylon blend adds durability for socks. A cotton-acrylic blend adds stretch to cotton's stiffness. Don't dismiss a yarn just because it isn't 100% one thing.

## How to substitute yarn in a pattern

Every knitter eventually falls in love with a pattern that calls for a discontinued yarn. Here's how to swap:

1. **Match the weight category first.** A DK pattern needs DK yarn.
2. **Check the gauge.** Knit a swatch with your substitute and compare. If the pattern says 22 stitches = 4 inches, your sub needs to hit that too.
3. **Consider the fibre.** Swapping wool for cotton changes the drape, stretch, and feel. It might work, but it won't be the same garment.
4. **Calculate yardage, not skeins.** Different brands put different amounts of yarn in each skein. Total the yardage the pattern requires and buy enough of your substitute to match.

## This week's tip

Keep a yarn notebook — or a note on your phone. Every time you buy yarn, jot down the brand, colourway, weight, and what you made with it. Over time, you build a personal reference that's more useful than any buying guide. You'll learn which brands you reach for and which ones sit in the stash untouched.
