## Your knitting isn't finished when you bind off

It's finished when you block it. That crumpled, slightly uneven thing on your needles? Blocking transforms it into the smooth, even, professional-looking piece you saw in the pattern photo.

If you've been skipping blocking, you've been skipping the step that makes everything else worth doing.

## What blocking actually does

Blocking is controlled moisture and shaping. It evens out your stitches, opens up lace patterns, relaxes tension inconsistencies, and sets your fabric to its final dimensions.

Think of it this way: knitting is sculpting the raw material. Blocking is the kiln firing. Without it, your piece is technically complete but hasn't reached its final form.

Practically, blocking does three things:

- **Evens out tension.** Even experienced knitters have slight variations in tension from row to row or stitch to stitch. Blocking smooths these out so your fabric looks uniform.
- **Opens up stitch patterns.** Lace, cables, and textured stitches often look compressed and muddled on the needles. Blocking lets each stitch settle into its proper shape.
- **Sets dimensions.** Your piece relaxes to its true gauge. This is why swatching (and blocking your swatch) matters — unblocked measurements lie.

## The three main methods

### Wet blocking

Best for: wool, alpaca, cotton, plant fibres, most blends.

1. Fill a basin with lukewarm water. Add a drop of wool wash if you like — Eucalan, Soak, or a splash of gentle shampoo all work.
2. Submerge your piece. Don't agitate it. Just let it soak for 15-20 minutes.
3. Lift it out gently — support its weight so it doesn't stretch. Press out the excess water against the side of the basin. Never wring.
4. Lay it flat on a towel. Roll the towel up like a swiss roll and press to absorb more water.
5. Unroll and lay the piece on blocking mats (interlocking foam tiles work brilliantly). Pin to measurements using T-pins or blocking pins.
6. Walk away. Let it dry completely. This takes 24-48 hours depending on thickness and climate.

Wet blocking is the most thorough method. If you only learn one, learn this one.

### Steam blocking

Best for: pieces that need a lighter touch, or when you're short on time.

1. Pin your piece to blocking mats, dry.
2. Hold a steam iron or garment steamer 2-3cm above the fabric. Never touch the iron to the knitting.
3. Let the steam penetrate the fibres, then let the piece cool and set.

Steam blocking works well for wool and natural fibres. It's faster than wet blocking but gives slightly less dramatic results.

**Warning:** Never steam acrylic. It will melt the fibres. Not a little — permanently.

### Spray blocking

Best for: quick touch-ups, small accessories, or pieces that just need minor evening out.

1. Pin your piece to blocking mats, dry.
2. Spritz thoroughly with a spray bottle of water. You want it damp, not soaked.
3. Let it dry.

Less effective than wet blocking but useful for scarves, dishcloths, or anything where precision isn't critical.

## Fibre-specific rules

**Wool and animal fibres** — wet or steam block. Warm water, gentle handling. These fibres are forgiving and respond beautifully to blocking. Superwash wool can grow when wet, so pin to finished measurements, not larger.

**Cotton and linen** — wet block. These fibres can handle more aggressive treatment. They don't have the memory of wool, so they may need re-blocking after washing. Cotton tends to grow lengthwise, so be aware of that when pinning.

**Acrylic** — "kill" it with steam held slightly farther from the fabric, or wet block with cold water and minimal handling. Acrylic doesn't absorb water the same way natural fibres do. Once you steam-set acrylic, it's permanent — the drape changes irreversibly. Test on a swatch first.

**Silk** — wet block with cool water, handle gently. Silk can lose its lustre if handled roughly when wet. Block flat, never hang.

**Blends** — follow the rules for the most delicate fibre in the blend. A wool-silk blend? Treat it like silk.

## The tools you actually need

You don't need to buy a blocking kit to start. Here's the minimum:

- **Blocking mats** — interlocking foam floor tiles from a hardware store work perfectly. You want a surface you can pin into.
- **T-pins or blocking pins** — rust-proof, sturdy. You'll use dozens for a shawl. Blocking wires are a luxury upgrade for straight edges.
- **A towel** — for rolling out excess water.
- **A spray bottle** — for spray blocking.
- **A tape measure** — pin to the dimensions in your pattern, not by eye.

If you knit regularly, blocking wires are worth the investment eventually. They turn a 30-minute pinning job into 5 minutes for any piece with long straight edges.

## Common mistakes

**Pinning before removing excess water.** If your piece is dripping wet, it's too heavy to hold its shape under pins. Roll it in a towel first.

**Stretching aggressively.** Block to the pattern measurements. Yanking lace open as far as it will go looks dramatic on the blocking mats but creates an overstretched, fragile fabric.

**Not blocking the swatch.** If you skip this, your gauge lies and your finished piece won't match the pattern dimensions. Block the swatch the way you'll block the finished piece.

**Impatience.** Unpinning before the piece is fully dry means it will relax back toward its unblocked state. Touch the thickest part. If it's even slightly damp, leave it.

## This week's exercise

Block something. Anything. A dishcloth, a swatch, a scarf that's been sitting in a drawer. Wet block it properly — soak, roll, pin, wait. Compare before and after. That's the moment blocking stops being optional.
