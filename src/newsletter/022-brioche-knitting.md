## What makes brioche different

In standard ribbing, you knit the knits and purl the purls. In brioche, every stitch is worked together with a yarn-over from the previous row. This creates paired columns of stitches that are twice as thick as normal knitting, producing a fabric that's squishy, insulating, and fully reversible.

Both sides look like knit ribbing. There is no wrong side.

## The essential moves

Brioche has its own vocabulary, but it breaks down to two moves:

**BRK (brioche knit):** Knit the stitch together with its paired yarn-over from the previous row. This treats two loops as one stitch.

**BRP (brioche purl):** Purl the stitch together with its paired yarn-over.

**SL1YO (slip 1, yarn over):** Slip a stitch purlwise with the yarn in front, then bring the yarn over the needle to the back. This creates the yarn-over that will be worked together with the slipped stitch on the next row.

That's it. Every brioche pattern is built from these three moves.

## Setting up: the foundation row

Before you start the brioche rhythm, you need a setup row:

1. Cast on an odd number of stitches.
2. **Setup row:** *K1, SL1YO* — repeat to the last stitch, K1.

This establishes the alternating pattern: one active stitch, one slipped-with-yarn-over pair. From here, every row follows the same sequence.

## The basic brioche rhythm

**Row 1:** *SL1YO, BRK* across, end with SL1YO.
**Row 2:** *BRK, SL1YO* across, end with BRK.

Repeat these two rows. Once you've done a few repeats, the rhythm clicks and you stop thinking about individual stitches. The yarn-overs drape naturally over the slipped stitches, and the BRK merges them smoothly.

## Two-colour brioche

This is where brioche gets exciting. Use two colours (one for each row) and each colour forms its own set of columns. The result is a fabric where one colour dominates on each side — flip the work over and the colours reverse.

**How it works:** Knit Row 1 with Colour A. Don't turn the work — slide it back to the other end of the needle (use circulars or double-pointed needles). Knit Row 2 with Colour B. Now turn the work. Repeat.

Each colour only works its own columns. Colour A makes the BRK stitches on one side; Colour B makes them on the other. The effect is striking — two distinct fabrics, interlocked.

**Yarn choice tip:** Pick two colours with strong contrast. Brioche mutes subtle colour differences because the stitches nestle into each other. Bold contrast makes the stitch definition pop.

## Reading your brioche

Brioche fabric looks dense and can be hard to read at first. Key things to look for:

- **Each "stitch" is actually a pair** — the stitch and its yarn-over sitting together on the needle.
- **Count columns, not individual loops.** If you cast on 21 stitches, you have 11 active columns and 10 slipped columns.
- **A dropped yarn-over** leaves a visible hole in the column. If you see a gap, check whether a yarn-over slipped off without being worked.

## Counting rows

Brioche rows look different from regular rows. Each visible "ridge" on one side represents two actual rows of work (because each row only works half the stitches). When a pattern says "work 20 rows," count 10 ridges on one side.

In two-colour brioche, one colour's ridges stack on one side, the other colour's on the reverse. Count the ridges of one colour to track your row count.

## Common mistakes

**Forgetting the yarn-over after a slip.** The yarn needs to come over the needle to create the pairing. If you just slip without the yarn-over, you'll have a naked stitch on the next row that can't be BRK'd properly. Fix: tink back to the mistake and redo the SL1YO.

**Working a BRK without catching the yarn-over.** If you knit just the stitch and leave the yarn-over behind, you'll add an extra stitch. Always pick up both loops. If the yarn-over has fallen below the stitch, use your needle to lift it back into position before working the BRK.

**Tension too tight.** Brioche needs room to breathe. The yarn-overs should sit loosely on the needle so the paired stitches can merge easily. Go up 1–2 needle sizes from what you'd normally use for the yarn weight.

## Three starter projects

**1. One-colour brioche scarf.** Cast on 21 stitches in a worsted or bulky yarn. Work basic brioche until you run out of yarn. The double-thick, reversible fabric is perfect for winter. No colour management, no complications — just rhythm.

**2. Two-colour brioche cowl.** Cast on 61 stitches on a 16-inch circular needle. Join for working in the round. Use two contrasting colours to create a cowl that's one colour on the outside and the other on the inside. Brioche in the round is slightly different (BRK on one round, BRP on the next), but the principle is the same.

**3. Brioche hat.** Work a two-colour brioche tube, then decrease at the crown by working BRK decreases (slip 2 together with their yarn-overs, knit the next stitch, pass slipped stitches over). The decrease creates a spiraling top.

## Yarn and needle considerations

**Yarn:** Smooth, plump yarn shows brioche best. Avoid anything fuzzy or hairy — the stitches merge too much and you lose definition. Merino, BFL, and polished wools are ideal. Brioche uses approximately 50% more yarn than standard ribbing for the same dimensions.

**Needles:** Go up 1–2 sizes. Brioche on too-small needles is a fight. The yarn-overs crowd the needle and you lose the squishy character that makes brioche worth doing.

**Gauge:** Swatch in brioche — stockinette gauge will not help you. The fabric behaves completely differently. It's wider and shorter than you'd expect.

## Beyond basic brioche

Once the rhythm is natural, explore:

- **Brioche increases and decreases** — these create lace-like holes and shaping within the brioche fabric. Used for hats, shawls, and garments.
- **Syncopated brioche** — selectively working BRK or BRP in different positions to create patterns (zigzags, diamonds, waves) within the brioche fabric.
- **Multi-colour brioche** — three or more colours, with colour changes creating graphic patterns against the pillowy background.

Brioche has a reputation for being difficult. It's not — it's just different. The setup takes attention, but once you're in the rhythm, it's one of the most satisfying textures in knitting.
