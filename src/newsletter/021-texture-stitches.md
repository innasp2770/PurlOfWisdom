## The building blocks

Every texture stitch is made from two moves you already have: knit and purl. A knit stitch shows a smooth V on the front. A purl stitch shows a bump. Arrange those bumps differently across rows and you get patterns that look far more complex than the instructions behind them.

If you can knit and purl, you can do texture work. Full stop.

## Seed stitch (moss stitch)

The simplest texture: alternate knit one, purl one across the row, then on the next row, knit the purls and purl the knits. You're placing a bump next to every smooth stitch, creating a dense, pebbly fabric that lies completely flat.

**Why it matters:** Seed stitch doesn't curl. At all. This makes it the go-to edging for scarves, blankets, and any piece where you want clean, flat borders without the fuss of ribbing or a separate edging.

**British vs American naming:** In UK patterns, what Americans call seed stitch is often called moss stitch, and what Americans call moss stitch (a 2x2 variation) is called double moss stitch. Always check the written instructions, not just the name.

## Basketweave

Blocks of stockinette and reverse stockinette arranged in a checkerboard. Knit a set number of stitches, purl the same number, repeat — then after a set number of rows, swap the knits and purls. The result looks woven.

A 5x5 basketweave (five stitches wide, five rows tall per block) is the most common size. Smaller blocks create a tighter texture; larger blocks create a bolder, more graphic pattern.

**Cast-on tip:** Your stitch count needs to be a multiple of your block width. For a 5x5 basketweave, cast on a multiple of 10 (five knit + five purl per repeat).

## Broken rib

Standard ribbing alternates columns of knits and purls on every row. Broken rib changes one of those rows to all knit (or all purl), interrupting the vertical lines. The result is a fabric with the stretch of ribbing but a more textured, less structured appearance.

**Simplest version:**
- Row 1: *K1, P1* across
- Row 2: Knit all

That's it. Two rows. The broken row prevents the fabric from pulling in the way true ribbing does, so it works better for garment bodies where you want texture but not compression.

## Waffle stitch

A combination of knits, purls, and slipped stitches (or sometimes just knits and purls) that creates a grid of raised squares with sunken channels between them — like a breakfast waffle.

**Basic waffle (4-row repeat):**
- Row 1 (RS): *K1, P1* across
- Row 2 (WS): Purl all
- Row 3 (RS): *P1, K1* across
- Row 4 (WS): Purl all

The offset between rows 1 and 3 creates the waffled texture. This fabric is thick and squishy, making it ideal for dishcloths, spa cloths, and baby blankets.

## Linen stitch (woven stitch)

Alternate between knitting a stitch and slipping one with the yarn in front, then on the return row, purl the slipped stitches and slip the knit ones. The carried yarn floats across the slipped stitches, creating a dense, woven-looking fabric.

**What makes it special:** Linen stitch produces a fabric that drapes like cloth rather than knitting. It's flatter, firmer, and more structured. The trade-off: it eats yarn — expect to use roughly 30% more than stockinette for the same dimensions.

**Speed tip:** Linen stitch is slow. The constant yarn-front, yarn-back motion breaks your rhythm. Accept it. The fabric is worth the pace.

## Choosing the right texture

| Goal | Best texture |
|---|---|
| Flat, non-curling fabric | Seed stitch or garter stitch |
| Visual interest with minimal effort | Broken rib |
| Thick, absorbent fabric (dishcloths, washcloths) | Waffle stitch or basketweave |
| Structured, drapey fabric for garments | Linen stitch |
| Bold, graphic pattern | Large basketweave |
| Subtle, sophisticated surface | Moss stitch or sand stitch |

## Three starter projects

**1. Textured sampler scarf.** Cast on 35 stitches. Knit sections of seed stitch, basketweave, broken rib, and waffle stitch, separated by a few rows of garter stitch. You get a scarf and a reference sampler in one.

**2. Waffle stitch dishcloth.** Cast on 40 stitches in cotton yarn. Work the four-row waffle pattern until square. The texture scrubs well and the fabric stays flat after washing.

**3. Linen stitch market bag.** Work a rectangle in linen stitch, fold it in half, seam the sides, add handles. The dense, structured fabric holds its shape under the weight of groceries.

## Common mistakes and fixes

**Losing track of the pattern:** Texture stitches require you to read your knitting — look at the stitch below to decide whether the current stitch should be a knit or purl. If you see a V, you might need to purl over it (for seed stitch) or knit over it (for ribbing). Practice reading your fabric rather than counting rows.

**Tension inconsistency:** The shift between knit and purl changes your tension. If your purl stitches are looser (they usually are), try going down a needle size for purl-heavy sections, or consciously snug the yarn after each purl.

**Curling at edges:** Some textures still curl at cast-on and bind-off edges. Add a few rows of garter stitch or seed stitch as a border to keep things flat.

## Beyond the basics

Once you're comfortable combining knits and purls, look into:

- **Gansey patterns** — traditional fishing-village textures using only knit and purl, arranged into panels of diamonds, flags, zigzags, and trees. The patterns told stories about the knitter's home.
- **Shadow knitting (illusion knitting)** — alternating rows of stockinette and reverse stockinette that reveal a hidden image when viewed from an angle.
- **Architectural knitting** — using texture to create three-dimensional surfaces, folds, and geometric forms.

Texture stitches are proof that complexity doesn't require new skills. Just a fresh way of combining what you've already got.
