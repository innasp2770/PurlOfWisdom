## Why colour changes everything

There's a moment in every knitter's journey where solid-colour stockinette stops being enough. You want patterns. Motifs. That gorgeous yoke sweater with the tiny geometric shapes. You want colourwork.

The good news: colourwork is not nearly as hard as it looks. The skill ceiling is high, but the entry point is low. If you can knit and purl, you can work with multiple colours. This issue takes you from the simplest colour technique — stripes — all the way to stranded colourwork basics.

## Stripes: the gateway

Stripes are the easiest colourwork you can do. Change yarns at the beginning of a row, knit with the new colour, switch back when you want. That's it.

A few things make stripes look better:

**Even-row stripes are cleaner.** When you change colour every two rows (or any even number), both the old and new yarn end up on the same edge. You can carry the unused colour up the side without cutting it. Odd-row stripes force you to cut the yarn or carry it up the wrong side, which creates a visible jog in circular knitting.

**The jogless join.** In circular knitting, stripes create a visible step where the round begins. To fix this: on the first stitch of the second round in the new colour, lift the stitch below the first stitch onto the left needle and knit it together with the first stitch. This smooths the colour change into an almost invisible transition.

**Carry the yarn loosely.** When you carry an unused colour up the side for two or four rows, catch it gently behind the working yarn every couple of rows. Don't pull it tight — it'll pucker the edge.

**Weave in as you go.** If you're doing many colour changes, weave the tails in while knitting rather than leaving twenty loose ends to deal with later. Duplicate stitch or running the tail through the purl bumps on the wrong side both work well.

## Choosing stripe combinations

Stripe sequences sound simple, but the wrong combination looks busy or flat. A few guidelines:

- **Vary the widths.** Two-row stripes alternating evenly look like a rugby shirt. Mix widths — four rows of the main colour, two rows of the contrast, eight rows of main — and the pattern gets visual rhythm.
- **Limit the palette.** Two or three colours are plenty for most stripe patterns. Adding a fourth can work but requires a clear hierarchy — one dominant, one secondary, one accent.
- **Use value contrast.** Colours that differ in lightness (one dark, one light) read more clearly than colours of similar depth. Squint at your yarns. If they blur into one shade, they won't stripe well.
- **Test on the needle.** Colours look different knitted up than they do in the skein. Wind a few yards of each around a piece of card together, or knit a quick stripe swatch before committing.

## Stranded colourwork: the basics

Stranded colourwork — sometimes called Fair Isle, though purists reserve that term for the specific tradition from Fair Isle, Scotland — uses two colours per row. You knit with one colour and carry (strand) the other behind the work until it's needed.

The fabric this produces is thicker and warmer than single-colour knitting because the carried strands (called "floats") create a double layer on the wrong side. This is why traditional Fair Isle sweaters are so warm.

### The mechanics

1. **Hold both yarns.** There are several methods. The most common: hold the main colour in your right hand (English style) and the contrast in your left (Continental style). Or hold both in one hand, separating them with different fingers. There's no wrong way — pick whatever lets you maintain even tension.

2. **Knit with one, strand the other.** When the pattern calls for the main colour, knit with it and let the contrast float behind. When it calls for the contrast, pick up that yarn and knit with it. The unused yarn simply travels along the back of the work.

3. **Keep floats loose.** This is the single most important rule. If you pull your floats tight, the fabric puckers and loses elasticity. Spread the stitches on your right needle before switching colours so the float has enough slack to lie flat.

4. **Catch long floats.** If a colour isn't used for more than five stitches, the float gets too long and will snag on fingers when you put the garment on. Catch the float by twisting it with the working yarn every three to four stitches. On the right side, this should be invisible.

### Reading a colourwork chart

Stranded patterns are always shown as charts, not written instructions. Each square represents one stitch. Each colour gets a symbol or shade. Read the chart right to left for right-side rows (or every row if knitting in the round) and left to right for wrong-side rows in flat knitting.

Start at the bottom right. Work across. Move up one row. That's it.

Most patterns repeat a motif across the row. The chart shows one repeat, marked by bold vertical lines. Count your stitches to make sure you have the right multiple before casting on.

## Tension and dominance

In stranded colourwork, one colour will always sit slightly in front of the other — this is called colour dominance. The dominant colour appears more prominent in the finished fabric.

The yarn held lower (or in the left hand, if you're holding one in each) tends to be dominant. For most patterns, you want the pattern colour (the motif) to be dominant and the background to recede. Keep the pattern colour in the dominant position consistently throughout the project.

Tension in colourwork takes practice. Your first attempt will probably be tight. Most knitters need to go up one or two needle sizes for colourwork sections compared to their plain stockinette gauge. Swatch in pattern, not in plain knitting, to get accurate gauge.

## Managing ends

Colourwork generates more yarn ends than single-colour work. A few strategies:

- **Join new colours at the edge** when possible, so the tails are at the seam line.
- **Knit the tails in** as you go. Lay the tail over the working yarn and trap it every other stitch for an inch. This is faster than weaving in with a needle later and more secure.
- **Use Russian join or spit splice** for wool yarns. These create a join with no tails at all. The Russian join loops the two ends through each other; the spit splice felts the overlapping ends together with moisture and friction.
- **Leave tails at least 15 cm long.** Short tails are harder to weave in and more likely to work loose.

## Your first colourwork project

Don't start with a sweater yoke. Start with something flat and small:

- **A hat.** Simple colourwork hats with a single motif band are the classic first project. Small enough to finish quickly, worked in the round so you never purl with two colours.
- **Mittens.** Traditional Scandinavian mittens are all-over colourwork, but the small gauge means you only manage two colours across thirty or forty stitches. Very satisfying.
- **A cowl.** A short tube with a few rows of colourwork and plenty of plain rows between. Low commitment, high visual impact.

Avoid patterns with more than two colours per row for your first project. Two is manageable. Three or more requires a completely different technique (intarsia), which we'll cover another time.

## This week's exercise

Knit a stripe swatch. Cast on 30 stitches with your main colour. Knit 4 rows. Switch to a contrast colour and knit 2 rows. Switch back and knit 6 rows. Try a jogless join if you're working in the round.

If you've done stripes before, try a simple stranded swatch. Find a free colourwork chart — a small snowflake or heart motif — and knit it in two colours. Focus on keeping the floats relaxed. Don't worry about perfection. The goal is to feel both yarns moving through your hands.
