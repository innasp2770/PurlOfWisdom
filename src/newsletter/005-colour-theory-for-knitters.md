## You already know more about colour than you think

Every time you pick a skein off the shelf because it "goes with" another one, you're doing colour theory. You just aren't calling it that.

The problem isn't that knitters lack colour instinct — it's that yarn shops put thousands of options in front of you, and instinct gets overwhelmed by choice. A little structure goes a long way.

## The colour wheel, kept simple

You don't need to memorise anything. Just picture three relationships:

**Complementary** — colours opposite each other on the wheel. Red and green. Blue and orange. Purple and yellow. These create high contrast and energy. In colourwork, complementary pairs make motifs pop. But use them in equal amounts and they vibrate against each other — let one dominate and the other accent.

**Analogous** — colours that sit next to each other. Teal, blue, and violet. Rust, red, and pink. These feel calm and harmonious. An analogous palette is nearly impossible to get wrong, which makes it a great default for multicolour projects.

**Triadic** — three colours evenly spaced around the wheel. Red, yellow, blue. Orange, green, purple. These are bold and cheerful. Think of them for playful projects — kids' items, accessories, anything that should feel lively.

## Value matters more than hue

Here is the single most important thing about colour in knitting: **contrast comes from value, not colour.**

Value means lightness vs. darkness. A pale pink and a pale blue look different on the shelf but nearly identical once they're knitted into a pattern — because they have the same value. Your carefully charted colourwork turns into a blur.

Want proof? Take a photo of your yarns and convert it to black and white. If the greys look distinct, your contrast is fine. If they blend together, swap one yarn for something lighter or darker.

## The squint test

This is the fastest colour tool you'll ever learn. Hold your yarn candidates together and squint. Your eyes will blur the details and show you the overall impression — does the combination feel balanced, or does one colour disappear?

Designers use this constantly. No phone needed, no apps, no swatches. Just squint.

## Warm, cool, and the sneaky neutral

Colours lean warm (yellow undertone) or cool (blue undertone), and mixing the two carelessly can feel off without you knowing why.

A warm red (tomato) next to a cool red (cherry) creates a subtle tension. That's not always bad — it adds depth — but if the clash bothers you, stick to one temperature family per project.

Neutrals aren't always neutral either. Grey can lean blue or brown. Cream can lean yellow or pink. Hold your "neutral" against a white sheet of paper to see its true undertone before committing.

## Building a palette in practice

Here's a process that works every time:

1. **Start with one skein you love.** This is your main colour. It gets the most yardage.
2. **Pick a second colour** in a clearly different value — lighter or darker. This is your contrast.
3. **Add a third colour** only if the pattern needs it. Choose something that shares an undertone with your main colour but sits at a different point on the value scale.
4. **Squint.** If all three are visible and nothing disappears, you're set.

For colourwork and stripes, two colours is often better than three. Simplicity reads well from a distance, which is where most people will see your finished piece.

## When colour goes wrong (and how to fix it)

Sometimes you're three inches into a project and the colours aren't working. That's not failure — it's information.

If two colours are too similar in value, swap one for a darker or lighter option. If a colour feels "off" but you can't say why, check its temperature against the others. And if the whole palette feels muddy, add one small pop of a bright or contrasting colour as an accent — even just a few rows or a single stripe can lift everything.

The cost of frogging a few inches now is nothing compared to finishing a project you don't love wearing.

## This week's tip

Next time you're in a yarn shop, pull five skeins that catch your eye and line them up. Take a black-and-white photo. You'll learn more about your colour instincts from that one exercise than from any theory.
