## Patterns aren't written to confuse you

They just look that way. Knitting patterns use a shorthand that evolved over decades to pack maximum information into minimum space. Once you learn the system, it reads like a recipe — which is exactly what it is.

The frustration most knitters feel isn't about intelligence. It's about notation. And notation can be learned in an afternoon.

## The anatomy of a pattern

Most patterns follow the same structure:

**Header** — yarn, needle size, gauge, finished measurements, notions. Read this first, even if you're tempted to skip ahead. The header tells you whether this pattern will work for you before you cast on a single stitch.

**Abbreviations list** — a key to every shorthand used in the pattern. Some are universal (k = knit, p = purl, st = stitch). Others are pattern-specific. Always check the abbreviation list, even if you think you know what everything means. One designer's "sl" might be another's "s1."

**Instructions** — the row-by-row or section-by-section directions. This is where the brackets and asterisks live.

**Finishing** — seaming, blocking, and assembly. Often the most important part and the most skipped.

## The abbreviations you'll see everywhere

You don't need to memorise all of them. These cover 90% of patterns:

- **k** — knit
- **p** — purl
- **st(s)** — stitch(es)
- **RS / WS** — right side / wrong side
- **rep** — repeat
- **tog** — together (as in k2tog: knit two stitches together)
- **yo** — yarn over
- **sl** — slip
- **pm / sm** — place marker / slip marker
- **BO / CO** — bind off / cast on
- **inc / dec** — increase / decrease
- **MC / CC** — main colour / contrast colour

When you hit an abbreviation you don't recognise, check the pattern's own list first. If it's not there, a quick search will sort it.

## Brackets, parentheses, and what lives inside them

Brackets mean "do this group of stitches as a unit." The number or instruction after the closing bracket tells you what to do with that unit.

**Example:** (k2, p2) 5 times

Translation: knit 2, purl 2 — and repeat that sequence 5 times total. That's 20 stitches.

**Nested brackets** work from inside out:

[(k1, p1) 3 times, k2] twice

Inner group: k1, p1 repeated 3 times = 6 stitches. Then k2 = 8 stitches. Do the whole thing twice = 16 stitches.

When brackets contain a stitch count at the end — (24 sts) — that's a checkpoint. Count your stitches. If you don't have 24, something went sideways. These are gifts from the designer. Don't ignore them.

## Asterisks: the repeat markers

Asterisks mark the start of a repeat section.

**Example:** *k3, p1; rep from * to last 2 sts, k2

Translation: knit 3, purl 1. Keep repeating that sequence. When you have 2 stitches left on the needle, knit them.

Some patterns use a single asterisk (*) for the main repeat and a double asterisk (**) for a secondary repeat nested inside. If you see both, read carefully — the double asterisk is a smaller loop inside the bigger one.

## Size ranges and the bracket game

Multi-size patterns put different numbers in brackets or parentheses, separated by commas or dashes:

**CO 80 (88, 96, 104, 112) sts**

If you're making the third size, your number is 96. Circle or highlight your size throughout the entire pattern before you start. Every single instance. This takes ten minutes and saves hours of frogging.

Some knitters photocopy the pattern and highlight their size in colour. Others use a PDF app and a digital highlighter. Either works. The point is: make your size impossible to miss.

## Charts vs. written instructions

Some patterns include both. Others offer only one.

**Written instructions** read left to right, but you knit the RS rows right to left and WS rows left to right. This trips people up. The instructions describe what you do, not the direction your needles travel.

**Charts** are a grid where each square is a stitch and each row is a row. Read charts from the bottom up. RS rows read right to left (the direction you're actually knitting). WS rows read left to right. Each symbol maps to a stitch type — the chart legend is non-negotiable reading.

If charts intimidate you, start with a simple one — a 4-row, 8-stitch repeat. Once the spatial logic clicks, charts become faster to follow than written instructions.

## The tricks that make pattern-reading easier

**Use a row counter.** Physical, app, or tally marks on paper. Doesn't matter. You will lose your place without one, and finding it again costs time and sanity.

**Read the whole pattern before casting on.** Not to memorise it — to spot surprises. "At the same time" instructions are notorious for hiding in the middle of a paragraph. If a pattern says "at the same time, begin neckline shaping," you need to know that's coming before you get there.

**Mark your progress.** A sticky note under the current row, a magnetic board, a line of washi tape across a printed page. Anything that answers "where was I?" in one glance.

**Trust the stitch counts.** When the pattern gives you a number in parentheses at the end of a section, count. Every time. It's your safety net.

## When the pattern seems wrong

Sometimes it is. Errata happen. Before assuming you made a mistake:

1. Re-read the instruction slowly, word by word.
2. Check the designer's website for corrections.
3. Search Ravelry for the pattern — other knitters often flag errors in the comments.
4. If it still doesn't work, you're probably not wrong. Move on with what makes sense and note the discrepancy.

## This week's exercise

Pick a pattern you've been intimidated by. Don't knit it — just read it. Go through the abbreviations, highlight your size, and translate the first ten rows into plain English. You'll be surprised how much clearer it gets when you slow down and decode it.
