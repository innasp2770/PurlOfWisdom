## Every knitter has a stash problem

It starts innocently. A sale on that beautiful hand-dyed merino. A souvenir skein from a trip. The leftover yarn from a finished project that's "too nice to throw away." Before you know it, you have three tote bags, a shelf, and a corner of the wardrobe all dedicated to yarn you haven't used yet.

A stash isn't a problem. An unmanageable stash is. The difference is knowing what you have, where it is, and what you plan to do with it.

## Step 1: Get everything in one place

You cannot organise what you cannot see. Pull every skein out of every bag, basket, drawer, and cupboard. Pile it all on the bed or the floor. Yes, all of it. Including the yarn you forgot you had.

This is the part where most people realise they own more yarn than they thought. That's fine. No judgement. We're here to make sense of it, not feel bad about it.

## Step 2: Sort by weight and fibre

Forget colour for now. Group your yarn by weight first — lace, fingering, sport, DK, worsted, bulky. Within each weight group, separate by fibre type: wool, cotton, acrylic, blends, novelty.

This sorting does two things. First, it tells you what you actually have to work with. Second, it shows you your buying patterns. If you own twelve skeins of fingering-weight merino and one ball of bulky cotton, that tells you something about the kind of knitter you are and the kind of projects you should be planning.

## Step 3: Check what's usable

Not all stash yarn is created equal. Look for:

- **Full ball bands.** If you don't know the weight, fibre content, or yardage, you'll struggle to use it. Write the details on a label and tuck it inside the band if the original is gone. If you truly can't identify it, set it aside — we'll deal with mystery yarn in a moment.
- **Enough yardage for a project.** A single leftover skein of DK probably isn't enough for a garment, but it's perfect for a hat, a dishcloth, or colourwork accents. Be honest about what you'll realistically make with it.
- **Condition.** Has it been stored near moths? Does it smell musty? Is it felted or tangled beyond redemption? Some yarn has passed its useful life. That's okay. Let it go.

## Dealing with mystery yarn

We all have it. That gorgeous skein with no ball band and no memory of where it came from.

The burn test helps identify fibre: snip a short length, hold it with tweezers, and bring a flame to the end. Wool smells like burning hair and leaves a crushable ash. Cotton smells like burning paper and leaves a light ash. Acrylic melts, smells chemical, and leaves a hard bead. Blends give mixed results.

For weight, wrap the yarn around a ruler for one inch and count the wraps. Compare to a wraps-per-inch chart: 14+ wraps = fingering, 11–12 = DK, 8–9 = worsted, 6–7 = bulky. This won't be exact, but it gets you close enough to swatch.

## Step 4: Choose a storage system

The best storage system is the one you'll actually maintain. Some options:

**Clear bins.** Stackable, visible, protect against dust and moths. Sort by weight or project. Label the outside.

**Ziplock bags.** Good for individual projects or groupings. Squeeze the air out. Toss in a cedar ring or lavender sachet for moth protection.

**Shelving.** Beautiful if you have the space. Keep yarn out of direct sunlight — UV fades colours and degrades fibres over time. Wool is particularly vulnerable.

**Project bags.** Assign a bag to each planned project with the pattern, yarn, and needles together. When you're ready to start, everything is in one place.

**What doesn't work:** loose piles, open bags on the floor, and "I'll remember where I put it." You won't. Future you deserves better.

## Step 5: Build a stash inventory

A spreadsheet, a note on your phone, or a Ravelry stash page — pick whatever you'll actually update. For each yarn, record:

- Yarn name and colourway
- Weight and fibre content
- Yardage per skein and number of skeins
- Where you bought it (helpful for reordering)
- Intended project, if any

This sounds tedious. It is, the first time. After that, adding a new purchase takes thirty seconds and saves you from buying a fourth skein of yarn you already own three of.

## Step 6: Match stash to projects

Now the fun part. Go through your queue of patterns and match them to yarn you already own. Use what we covered last week about yarn substitution — check weight, fibre suitability, and yardage.

You'll find that some yarn has an obvious purpose. Some has no purpose and probably never will. And some has been waiting for the right pattern to come along.

For the yarn with no purpose and no plan: set a deadline. If you haven't found a project for it in six months, gift it, donate it, or sell it. Stash that just sits there isn't an asset. It's clutter with sentimental value.

## The "one in, one out" rule

This isn't about deprivation. It's about sustainability. Before you buy new yarn, ask:

1. What project is this for?
2. Do I already own something that would work?
3. Where will I store it?

If you can answer all three, buy the yarn with zero guilt. If you can't, maybe browse the stash instead of the shop.

## When the stash is the project

Some knitters' greatest skill is curating a beautiful yarn collection. If that brings you joy, own it. Not every skein needs to become a scarf. But know the difference between "I'm collecting" and "I'm avoiding." If the stash is growing because starting projects feels overwhelming, that's a different conversation — one worth having honestly with yourself.

## This week's exercise

Inventory five skeins from your stash. Just five. Record the details, check the condition, and assign a project or a deadline to each one. Five skeins takes fifteen minutes and builds the habit.
