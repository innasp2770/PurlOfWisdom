## Why charts exist

Written instructions describe your knitting one stitch at a time, left to right, row by row. A chart *shows* your knitting as a grid — each square is a stitch, each row of squares is a row of knitting. The finished chart looks like the finished fabric.

For simple patterns, written instructions work fine. For anything with repeats, shaping, or colourwork, charts compress pages of text into a single image. A cable panel that takes ten lines of written instructions fits in a two-inch chart.

## How to read a chart

**Right side (RS) rows:** Read right to left — the same direction you knit them.

**Wrong side (WS) rows:** Read left to right.

**Row numbers:** Usually printed on the side where you start reading. RS rows numbered on the right, WS rows on the left.

**Bottom to top:** Row 1 is at the bottom of the chart. You read upward, just as your knitting grows upward.

**In the round:** Every row reads right to left, because you're always looking at the right side.

## The essential symbols

Every publisher uses slightly different symbols, but these are nearly universal:

| Symbol | RS meaning | WS meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Empty square or · | Knit | Purl |
| – or • | Purl | Knit |
| O | Yarn over | Yarn over |
| / | K2tog (right-leaning decrease) | P2tog |
| \ | SSK (left-leaning decrease) | SSP |
| X | Cable (varies — check the key) | Cable (check key) |

**The golden rule:** Always read the chart key first. Every pattern should include one. If it doesn't, look up the publisher's standard symbol chart.

## Repeats and boundaries

Charts use bold vertical lines or brackets to mark the **repeat section** — the stitches you'll work multiple times across the row. Stitches outside the brackets are worked once at the beginning or end of the row (edge stitches).

**Example:** A chart shows 3 stitches, then a bracketed section of 8 stitches, then 3 more. You'd work the first 3, repeat the 8-stitch section as many times as your stitch count allows, then work the final 3.

Your cast-on number must accommodate: edge stitches + (repeat × N).

## Colourwork charts

Colourwork charts use colour instead of symbols. Each coloured square means "knit this stitch in this colour." The chart is a pixel map of your fabric.

**Stranded colourwork (Fair Isle):** Two colours per row. The unused colour floats behind the work. Chart reading is the same — right to left on RS rows.

**Intarsia:** Separate blocks of colour, each with its own yarn supply. The chart shows colour boundaries. At each boundary, twist the yarns to prevent holes.

**Tip:** Print colourwork charts in colour if possible. Trying to distinguish between symbols for five different colours in a black-and-white chart is an exercise in frustration.

## Charts for different stitch types

**Lace charts:** Heavy on yarn-overs (O) and decreases (/ and \). The yarn-overs and decreases should balance — every yarn-over adds a stitch, every decrease removes one. If the stitch count stays constant, they're paired. If it changes, the chart should note it.

**Cable charts:** Use wider symbols that span multiple columns. A 6-stitch cable symbol occupies 6 squares across. The symbol itself usually indicates direction (left-leaning or right-leaning). Cable charts often show only the RS rows, with a note that WS rows are worked as they appear.

**Texture charts:** The simplest — just knits and purls in different arrangements. Once you learn the two basic symbols, you can read any texture chart.

## Marking your place

Charts demand tracking. Lose your place and you'll misread the pattern.

**Magnetic board and strips:** Place the chart on a metal board, use a magnetic strip to mark your current row. Move it up after each row.

**Sticky notes:** Place one just above your current row so you can see the row you're working plus all completed rows below it. Never cover the rows below — you need them to verify your work.

**Row counter apps:** Knit Companion, StitchFiddle, or even a simple tally app. Tap after each row. Some apps let you load charts and highlight your current row digitally.

**Highlighter method:** For printed charts, use a highlighter to mark completed rows. Irreversible but effective.

## Converting written instructions to charts

Sometimes you'll want to chart a written pattern for clarity. The process:

1. Grid paper or a charting app (StitchFiddle is excellent for this).
2. Start at Row 1 (bottom right of the grid).
3. Read the written instruction and fill in the corresponding symbols, right to left.
4. Move up one row. If it's a WS row, remember the symbols reverse (a knit in the written instruction becomes a blank/knit symbol on the chart, but it'll be read as purl from the RS).

**Shortcut:** Most charting software handles the RS/WS conversion automatically. Input what you do on each row and the chart reflects the RS appearance.

## Common chart-reading mistakes

**Reading RS rows left to right.** RS rows go right to left. The first stitch you knit is the rightmost square. If your pattern looks mirrored, you're reading backwards.

**Ignoring the WS symbol meaning.** A blank square means knit on the RS and purl on the WS. If you knit it on both sides, you'll get garter stitch instead of stockinette. Always check what the symbol means for the side you're on.

**Miscounting repeats.** Place a stitch marker after each repeat section on your needles. If you end a repeat and you're not at the marker, you've miscounted somewhere in that section.

**Skipping the key.** A circle might mean yarn-over in one pattern and bobble in another. Never assume — read the key.

## Three starter exercises

**1. Chart a dishcloth pattern.** Take any simple texture pattern (seed stitch, basketweave) and chart it on graph paper. Then knit from the chart instead of the written instructions. Compare the experience.

**2. Follow a free lace chart.** Find a simple lace pattern (many are available on Ravelry for free). Work one repeat, using a magnetic strip to track your row. Focus on reading the symbols rather than counting stitches.

**3. Chart your own colourwork.** On graph paper or StitchFiddle, design a simple motif — your initial, a heart, a star. Keep it under 15 stitches wide. Knit a swatch from your chart using two colours.

Charts aren't harder than written instructions — they're a different language for the same thing. And for most stitch patterns, they're the clearer one.
