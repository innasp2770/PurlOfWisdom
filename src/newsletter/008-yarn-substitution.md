## The yarn in the pattern isn't the only yarn that works

Patterns are written with a specific yarn, but they're really written for a specific gauge, weight, and drape. Match those three things and you can use almost anything you want.

Yarn substitution is one of the most empowering skills in knitting. It takes you from "I can only make what's listed" to "I can make this in any yarn I choose." That's a different relationship with patterns entirely.

## Why you'd substitute

- The recommended yarn is discontinued
- It's out of your budget
- You can't get it locally or the shipping is prohibitive
- You have something in your stash that could work
- You want a different fibre for allergies, climate, or care requirements
- You just don't like it

All valid. The pattern designer chose a yarn they loved. You get to choose one you love.

## Step 1: Match the weight

This is the non-negotiable starting point. If the pattern calls for a worsted weight yarn, you need a worsted weight substitute. A DK or bulky won't give you the same fabric no matter what needle size you use.

Check the ball band for the weight category (the number in the little yarn skein symbol) or look at the recommended gauge. If the pattern says "20 stitches and 26 rows = 10cm on 4mm needles," that's your target. Find a yarn that hits similar numbers on the same needles.

If you're not sure about a yarn's weight, the manufacturer's recommended gauge on the ball band is more reliable than the weight name. "DK" means different things to different brands.

## Step 2: Consider the fibre

Different fibres behave differently, even at the same weight. This is where most substitution problems happen.

**Wool** has memory — it bounces back. A ribbed cuff in wool will snap back to shape. The same cuff in cotton will stretch out and stay stretched.

**Cotton** has no memory but great stitch definition. It's heavier than wool and drapes differently. A garment designed in wool will hang and fit differently in cotton, even at identical gauge.

**Acrylic** varies hugely by brand. Budget acrylic can feel plasticky and pill. Good acrylic (like some of the newer micro-fibre blends) can be soft and durable. It won't block the same way as natural fibres.

**Alpaca** is warm, drapey, and grows. A sweater knit in alpaca instead of wool will be longer and looser after wearing. Size down if you're substituting alpaca for wool.

**Linen and hemp** are stiff at first and soften dramatically with washing. The first wearing won't feel like the tenth. They also have zero elasticity, so anything that relies on stretch (socks, fitted cuffs) won't work well.

**Silk** adds drape and sheen but no elasticity. Beautiful for shawls, tricky for structured garments.

The rule: if you're changing fibre type, expect the fabric to behave differently even if your gauge swatch matches. Knit a swatch big enough to feel the drape, not just count the stitches.

## Step 3: Swatch honestly

You knew this was coming. Swatch in your substitute yarn on the pattern's recommended needles. Block the swatch (we covered this last week). Then measure.

If your gauge matches, you're clear to proceed. If it's close but not exact, try going up or down a needle size. If it's way off, this yarn probably isn't the right substitute for this pattern.

Two swatches that measure the same can still feel different. Hold your swatch up and let it drape. Does it feel like the fabric you want next to your skin? Is it too stiff? Too limp? Gauge is necessary but not sufficient. Trust your hands.

## Step 4: Calculate yardage, not weight

Patterns list yarn requirements in grams or skeins, but what you actually need is yardage (or meterage). A 100g skein of fingering weight has far more yards than a 100g skein of bulky.

Look up the yards per gram of the original yarn and your substitute. If the original has 200 yards per 100g and your substitute has 150 yards per 100g, you'll need more skeins of the substitute to get the same total yardage.

Formula: (pattern's total yards needed) ÷ (yards per skein of your substitute) = skeins needed. Round up. Always round up. Running out of yarn two rows from the end is a special kind of heartbreak.

## Step 5: Check the care requirements

This matters more than people think. If the pattern assumes a machine-washable superwash wool and you substitute with a hand-wash-only merino, you've built a garment that needs different care than the designer intended.

This isn't a dealbreaker — just something to know before you commit. Baby items, everyday accessories, and anything that gets heavy use should be in a yarn that matches how you'll actually care for it.

## Substitution cheat sheet

| If the pattern uses... | Good substitutes | Watch out for... |
|---|---|---|
| Superwash merino | Other superwash wools, merino blends | Acrylic won't block the same |
| Cotton | Linen, cotton blends, bamboo | Will be heavier — check finished weight |
| Alpaca | Merino-alpaca blends, cashmere | Pure alpaca grows — swatch and hang |
| Acrylic | Other acrylics, acrylic-wool blends | Quality varies wildly by brand |
| Silk | Silk blends, tencel, bamboo silk | Drape will differ — test it |

## When substitution won't work well

Some patterns depend on specific yarn qualities that are hard to replicate:

- **Colourwork patterns** designed for sticky, non-superwash wool rely on the fibres gripping each other. Substituting slippery yarn (silk, bamboo) means your floats may show through.
- **Lace patterns** designed for wool rely on blocking to open up. Cotton lace won't bloom the same way.
- **Structural patterns** (bags, baskets) need a firm yarn. Substituting a soft, drapey yarn defeats the purpose.

In these cases, stay closer to the original yarn's characteristics. Match not just the weight but the behaviour.

## This week's exercise

Pick a pattern in your queue and look up the recommended yarn. Find two possible substitutes — one in a similar fibre and one in a different fibre. Compare yards per gram, gauge, and care instructions. You don't have to buy anything. The exercise is in learning to evaluate.
