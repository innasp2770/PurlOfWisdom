## Why socks

Socks are the best project in knitting. That's not an exaggeration — they teach you more techniques per square centimetre than almost anything else, they're portable, they use small amounts of yarn, and the result is something genuinely useful.

A single pair of top-down socks will teach you knitting in the round, decreases, short rows, picking up stitches, and grafting. If you can knit a sock, you can knit almost anything.

They're also addictive. Fair warning.

## What you need

**Yarn:** Fingering weight (4-ply) sock yarn. Look for something with at least 20% nylon blended with wool or superwash merino — the nylon adds durability where you need it most. One 100g skein (roughly 400 metres) makes one pair of average adult socks.

**Needles:** 2.25mm or 2.5mm. You have three options:
- **Double-pointed needles (DPNs):** The traditional method. Four or five short needles forming a triangle or square. Fiddly at first, satisfying once you get the hang of it.
- **Magic loop:** One long circular needle (80cm or longer). You fold the cable in half and work sections in turn. Fewer needles to manage, but more cable adjusting.
- **Two circulars:** Two shorter circular needles (40cm each). The middle ground. Less fiddly than DPNs, less cable wrangling than magic loop.

Pick whichever feels least intimidating. You can always switch methods later.

**Extras:** Stitch markers, a tapestry needle for grafting, and a row counter if you like one.

## Anatomy of a sock

A top-down sock has five parts, knitted in this order:

1. **Cuff:** The ribbed top that keeps the sock up. Usually 2.5–5cm of 1×1 or 2×2 rib.
2. **Leg:** The section from cuff to heel. Can be plain stockinette or patterned. Length is personal preference.
3. **Heel flap and turn:** A small rectangular flap worked back and forth, then shaped with short rows to create the cup that wraps around your heel.
4. **Gusset:** Stitches picked up along the edges of the heel flap, then decreased back to the original stitch count. This creates the diagonal shaping on each side of the foot.
5. **Foot and toe:** Stockinette in the round until you reach the toe, then decreases shape the end to a point or rounded tip, finished with Kitchener stitch to close it seamlessly.

## Casting on and the cuff

Cast on 64 stitches (adjust for your gauge — a standard women's sock at 32 stitches per 10cm in stockinette uses 64 stitches). Use a long-tail cast on for stretch, or a German twisted cast on if you want extra elasticity.

Distribute the stitches across your DPNs or arrange them for magic loop. Join to work in the round, being careful not to twist. Place a marker at the start of the round.

Work 2×2 rib (knit 2, purl 2) for about 2.5cm. Some knitters prefer longer cuffs — go as long as you like.

## The leg

Switch to stockinette (knit every round) and work until the leg reaches your desired length from the top of the cuff. For an average ankle sock, 5–7cm of leg is comfortable. For a crew sock, 12–15cm.

If you want a pattern on the leg — a simple rib, a texture stitch, a small cable — this is where it goes. Keep the sole half in plain stockinette to reduce bulk inside your shoe.

## The heel flap

This is where you stop working in the round and start working back and forth on half your stitches.

Put 32 stitches on one needle (or one half of your magic loop) — these are your heel stitches. The other 32 stitches rest and wait (the instep stitches).

**Row 1 (RS):** *Slip 1 purlwise, knit 1.* Repeat across all 32 heel stitches.
**Row 2 (WS):** Slip 1 purlwise, purl to end.

Repeat these two rows until your heel flap is roughly square — usually 32 rows (16 slipped stitches along each edge). The slipped-stitch pattern creates a dense, durable fabric right where your shoe rubs, and those slipped edge stitches become neat pickup points for the gusset.

## Turning the heel

This is the part that feels like magic. You're shaping the flat flap into a three-dimensional cup using short rows — rows that don't go all the way across.

**Row 1 (RS):** Knit 18, SSK, knit 1, turn. (You've left stitches unworked at the end.)
**Row 2 (WS):** Slip 1, purl 5, P2tog, purl 1, turn.
**Row 3 (RS):** Slip 1, knit to 1 stitch before the gap, SSK (closing the gap), knit 1, turn.
**Row 4 (WS):** Slip 1, purl to 1 stitch before the gap, P2tog (closing the gap), purl 1, turn.

Repeat rows 3 and 4 until all the side stitches have been consumed. You'll end up with about 18 stitches on the heel needle.

Look at the heel. It cups. That's the short rows doing their work — each row ate one more stitch from the sides, pulling the fabric into shape.

## The gusset

Now you rejoin for working in the round, but first you need to pick up stitches along the edges of the heel flap.

**Pick up and knit:** Along the left side of the heel flap, pick up 16 stitches (one in each slipped edge stitch). Knit across the 32 instep stitches. Pick up 16 stitches along the right side of the heel flap. Knit across the heel stitches.

You now have more stitches than you started with (about 82 instead of 64). That's correct — the gusset decreases will bring you back down.

**Gusset decreases:**
**Round 1:** Knit to 3 stitches before the instep, K2tog, knit 1. Knit across instep. Knit 1, SSK, knit to end of round.
**Round 2:** Knit all stitches.

Repeat these two rounds until you're back to 64 stitches total. The decreases create the diagonal lines you see on the sides of a hand-knit sock.

## The foot

Knit in the round on all 64 stitches until the sock reaches from the back of the heel to about 5cm before the tip of the longest toe. Try it on if you can (carefully, with the stitches on a circular needle or cable). If you can't try it on, measure the intended foot length and subtract 5cm.

## Shaping the toe

**Round 1:**
- Needle 1 (sole): Knit 1, SSK, knit to last 3 stitches, K2tog, knit 1.
- Needle 2 (instep): Knit 1, SSK, knit to last 3 stitches, K2tog, knit 1.

(Four decreases per round — two on the sole side, two on the instep side.)

**Round 2:** Knit all stitches.

Repeat these two rounds until you have about 20–24 stitches remaining (10–12 on each needle).

## Kitchener stitch (grafting)

This is the invisible join that closes the toe. It looks intimidating written down, but once you've done it twice it becomes muscle memory.

Set up: Cut the yarn leaving a 30cm tail. Thread it on a tapestry needle. Hold the two sets of stitches parallel on two needles, wrong sides together, yarn coming from the back needle.

**The four-step mantra:**

1. Tapestry needle through the first stitch on the front needle as if to purl. Leave the stitch on.
2. Tapestry needle through the first stitch on the back needle as if to knit. Leave the stitch on.
3. Tapestry needle through the first stitch on the front needle as if to knit. Slip it off.
4. Tapestry needle through the next stitch on the front needle as if to purl. Leave it on.
5. Tapestry needle through the first stitch on the back needle as if to purl. Slip it off.
6. Tapestry needle through the next stitch on the back needle as if to knit. Leave it on.

Repeat steps 3–6 until all stitches are grafted. Adjust the tension of the grafting yarn to match the surrounding stitches — too tight and you get a pucker, too loose and you get a visible line.

The toe is seamless. No seam to rub against your toes.

## Second sock syndrome

You will finish your first sock, hold it up in triumph, and then look at the remaining yarn and feel absolutely no motivation to start the second one. This is universal. It has a name: second sock syndrome.

Strategies:
- **Two at a time:** Knit both socks simultaneously on one long circular needle. Eliminates the problem entirely, though the setup takes practice.
- **Immediately cast on:** Don't put the first sock down. Cast on the second one right away while the pattern is fresh.
- **Different yarn:** Some knitters keep a second project going and alternate. The variety maintains momentum.
- **Accept it:** Some first socks wait weeks for their partner. The sock won't mind.

## Fit adjustments

The 64-stitch, fingering-weight formula works for many adult feet, but not all. Here's how to adjust:

- **Wider foot:** Add stitches in multiples of 4. Try 68 or 72.
- **Narrower foot:** Subtract in multiples of 4. Try 60 or 56.
- **High instep:** Pick up a few extra gusset stitches and add extra decrease rounds.
- **Short or long foot:** Simply knit fewer or more rounds before starting the toe.

The beauty of hand-knit socks is that they can fit exactly. Commercial socks fit approximately.
