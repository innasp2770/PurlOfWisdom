## Why this matters more than any stitch

You can learn every stitch in every book, but if you can't look at your knitting and understand what's happening, you'll always be guessing. Reading your knitting means recognising what each stitch looks like on the needle and in the fabric — so you know where you are, what went wrong, and what comes next without constantly checking the pattern.

This is the skill that turns a nervous beginner into a relaxed knitter. It's also the skill most tutorials skip because it's hard to teach in a photo. But once it clicks, everything else gets easier.

## Anatomy of a stitch

Every knitted stitch is a loop pulled through another loop. That's it. The entire craft is loops through loops. But the direction, orientation, and arrangement of those loops create different fabrics.

**Knit stitches** look like tiny V shapes on the side facing you. Rows of V's stacked on top of each other give you stockinette — that smooth, classic knitted surface.

**Purl stitches** are the back of a knit stitch. They look like small horizontal bumps. If you turn stockinette over, you see all purls. Garter stitch (knit every row flat, or alternate knit/purl rounds in the round) shows alternating rows of V's and bumps.

**Yarn overs** are holes with a visible strand running diagonally beside them. In lace patterns, yarn overs create the decorative holes. If you see one where there shouldn't be one, you accidentally wrapped the yarn.

**Twisted stitches** look like V's with their legs crossed. The stitch sits on the needle with the leading leg behind instead of in front. They're tighter and slightly angled. Sometimes intentional (twisted rib looks sharp), but usually a sign that a stitch was mounted wrong after picking it up or that you're wrapping the yarn the wrong direction.

## Identifying stitches on the needle

When your stitches are sitting on the needle waiting to be worked, you can read them before you knit them.

**The leading leg rule:** A correctly mounted stitch has its right leg (the one closer to the tip) in front of the needle and its left leg behind. If the left leg is in front, the stitch is twisted — either mounted backward or wrapped in the wrong direction.

**How to check:** Slide a stitch toward the tip and look at which leg sits in front. If you're knitting English or Continental style in the standard way, the leading leg should always be the right one.

This matters most when you've just picked up a dropped stitch with a crochet hook or rearranged stitches. It's easy to put them back on the needle facing the wrong way. A quick check before knitting saves you from an accidental twist.

## Counting rows

In flat knitting, you can count rows from the edge. But in the round, or in the middle of a piece, you need to count from the fabric itself.

**Stockinette:** Each V is one row. Count the V columns vertically. Easier on the knit side than the purl side.

**Garter stitch:** Each ridge (the raised bump row) equals two rows. Count the ridges and multiply by two.

**Ribbing:** Count the V's in one knit column. Each V is one row.

**Seed stitch or moss stitch:** Harder to count visually. Use a row counter or tally marks. The fabric is intentionally irregular, which makes visual counting unreliable.

**Tip:** If you're counting a long section, place a removable stitch marker or a piece of scrap yarn every 10 or 20 rows as you go. Much easier than counting 80 rows of stockinette after the fact.

## Spotting mistakes in the fabric

The faster you catch a mistake, the less painful the fix. Here's what to look for:

**Dropped stitch:** A live loop sitting below the needles, not on them. In stockinette, it creates a visible ladder of horizontal bars running down the fabric. The longer it goes unnoticed, the further it runs. Catch it early and fix it with a crochet hook. Catch it late and you might need to rip back.

**Extra stitch:** Your stitch count increased. Usually caused by an accidental yarn over (yarn went over the needle between stitches) or by knitting into the bar between stitches instead of into a stitch. Count your stitches at the end of every row until you trust your tension.

**Missing stitch:** Your count decreased. A stitch slipped off without being worked, or you accidentally knit two together. If it just happened, you can tink (knit backward) to fix it. If it was several rows ago, you'll see the fabric pulling in at that point.

**Twisted stitch:** That crossed-leg V. One stitch isn't a disaster — it just looks slightly different. A whole column of them means your mount or wrap is consistently wrong.

**Split yarn:** You knitted through part of the ply instead of the whole strand. The stitch looks fuzzy or has a small loop of fibre hanging out. Tink back and reknit the stitch cleanly.

## Reading your knitting in pattern

When you're working a stitch pattern — cables, lace, textured stitches — you can use the fabric itself to know where you are in the repeat.

**Cables:** Count the rows since the last cable cross. You can see the twist row clearly. If your pattern says "cross every 8 rows" and you can count 6 rows of straight knitting above the last cross, you know you have 2 more rows before the next one.

**Lace:** The yarn overs (holes) and decreases create a visible structure. Compare what you see to the chart. If the holes align vertically in your pattern and you see one drifting sideways, something shifted.

**Ribbing:** The columns should be straight and continuous. If a knit column suddenly has a purl bump or vice versa, you worked the wrong stitch. Catch it while it's on the needle — one row back is an easy fix.

The goal is to look at your fabric more than your pattern. The pattern tells you the plan. The fabric tells you the truth.

## The tink skill

"Tink" is "knit" spelled backward, and that's exactly what it is — unknitting one stitch at a time. It's the safest way to go back a few stitches or a row without ripping.

**How to tink:**
1. Insert the left needle into the stitch below the one on the right needle, from front to back.
2. Slide the right needle out of the stitch.
3. The stitch is now back on the left needle, un-worked.
4. Repeat until you reach the mistake.

**When to tink vs. when to rip (frog):** Tink when the mistake is within a few stitches or a couple of rows. Rip when it's many rows back. Tinking 10 rows of lace will take longer than carefully ripping and picking up stitches from a lifeline.

## Building the habit

Every time you pick up your knitting:
1. Look at the fabric before you start. What row are you on? What side are you on? What stitch comes next?
2. After every row or round, glance at the last few stitches. Do they look right?
3. Count stitches at the end of every row until it becomes automatic.

This takes 10 seconds per row. It saves hours of ripping later.

Over time, you won't need to consciously check. You'll feel a mistake the moment it happens — the yarn didn't pull through cleanly, the stitch count feels off, the fabric looks wrong. That's reading your knitting becoming instinct.
